SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: Definition and Location of Magnetic Poles
DEFINITION:
Magnetic poles are the regions of a magnet where magnetic force is strongest, and every magnet has exactly two poles: North Pole and South Pole.

KEY_POINTS:

Every magnet, regardless of shape or size, has two poles.
North Pole is usually marked red or labeled "N".
South Pole is usually marked blue/black or labeled "S".

EXAMPLES:
When a bar magnet is dipped in iron filings, most filings cluster at the two ends and very few stick to the middle.

KEYWORDS:
magnetic pole, North Pole, South Pole, magnetic concentration, magnet ends

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: The First Law of Magnetism
DEFINITION:
The magnetic interaction rule states that like poles repel each other while unlike poles attract each other.

KEY_POINTS:

Two North poles push away from each other (repulsion).
Two South poles push away from each other (repulsion).
A North pole and South pole pull toward each other (attraction).

EXAMPLES:
Bringing two North poles close together creates a pushing force that keeps them separated.

KEYWORDS:
law of magnetism, repulsion, attraction, like poles, unlike poles

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Freely Suspended Magnet and Compass Principle
DEFINITION:
A magnet suspended freely by a thread will always align itself in a North-South direction because the Earth acts like a giant magnet.

KEY_POINTS:

The end pointing toward geographic North is the North-seeking pole.
The end pointing toward geographic South is the South-seeking pole.
This principle has been used by sailors, including Sri Lankan sailors across the Indian Ocean, for navigation.

EXAMPLES:
A suspended bar magnet always settles into a North-South orientation without external pushing.

KEYWORDS:
freely suspended magnet, compass, Earth magnetic field, navigation, Sri Lanka sailors

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C4
NAME: Magnetic Monopole Impossibility
DEFINITION:
A single isolated magnetic pole (monopole) cannot exist; breaking a magnet creates two new complete magnets, each with its own poles.

KEY_POINTS:

Breaking a magnet in half produces two magnets, not an isolated North and South piece.
Even tiny magnetic fragments have both North and South poles.
Complete magnets cannot be subdivided into isolated poles.

EXAMPLES:
Cutting a bar magnet lengthwise still produces two complete bar magnets, each with both poles.

KEYWORDS:
monopole, pole pairs, magnet splitting, atomic alignment, inseparable poles

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C5
NAME: Earth's Magnetic Poles versus Geographic Poles
DEFINITION:
Earth's Magnetic North Pole is located near the geographic South Pole, and Earth's Magnetic South Pole is near the geographic North Pole, causing compass needles to align differently than expected.

KEY_POINTS:

Earth acts as a giant bar magnet with buried poles.
Magnetic poles are reversed relative to geographic poles.
Unlike poles attract, so compass North poles are attracted to Earth's Magnetic South Pole.

EXAMPLES:
A compass needle's North pole points toward geographic North because it is attracted to Earth's Magnetic South Pole located there.

KEYWORDS:
Earth magnetic field, magnetic poles, geographic poles, compass alignment, reversed poles

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C6
NAME: Magnetic Induction and Temporary Poles
DEFINITION:
Magnetic induction is the process by which a strong permanent magnet induces temporary opposite poles in nearby magnetic materials.

KEY_POINTS:

Iron, steel, nickel, and cobalt become temporarily magnetized near a permanent magnet.
The induced pole closest to the magnet is always opposite.
Induced magnetism disappears when the permanent magnet is removed.

EXAMPLES:
An iron nail brought near a magnet's North pole develops a South pole at the tip and becomes attracted to the magnet.

KEYWORDS:
magnetic induction, temporary poles, magnetic materials, iron, induction chain

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C7
NAME: Pole Position in Different Magnet Shapes
DEFINITION:
The location of magnetic poles varies depending on the magnet's shape and design.

KEY_POINTS:

Bar magnets have poles at opposite ends.
Horseshoe magnets have both poles on the same side for greater lifting force.
Ring magnets typically have poles at opposite flat faces.
Cylindrical magnets have poles at the circular ends.

EXAMPLES:
A horseshoe magnet can lift heavier objects because both North and South poles on the same side can pull on a single object.

KEYWORDS:
bar magnet, horseshoe magnet, ring magnet, cylindrical magnet, pole position, magnet shape

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C8
NAME: Demagnetization and Pole Breakdown
DEFINITION:
Demagnetization is the loss of magnetic properties when atomic alignment inside a magnet is disrupted, causing poles to disappear.

KEY_POINTS:

Heat causes atoms to vibrate and lose organized alignment.
Physical shocks and hammering can jumble atomic order.
Improper storage with opposing poles touching can weaken magnets.

EXAMPLES:
A magnet heated to high temperature loses its ability to attract iron as atomic structure randomizes.

KEYWORDS:
demagnetization, atomic alignment, heat damage, physical shock, magnet storage, pole loss

SECTION: SUMMARY
Every magnet has two poles—a North Pole and a South Pole—where magnetic force is strongest, and these poles follow the First Law of Magnetism: like poles repel while unlike poles attract. A freely suspended magnet aligns itself along Earth's magnetic field, a principle used in compasses by Sri Lankan sailors for centuries to navigate the Indian Ocean. Magnetic poles always come in pairs, so breaking a magnet creates two new complete magnets rather than isolated poles. Earth's Magnetic South Pole is located near geographic North, which is why compass needles point northward due to attraction between unlike poles. Temporary poles can be induced in magnetic materials like iron through induction, but this magnetism disappears when the permanent magnet is removed. The position of poles varies by magnet shape—bar magnets have end poles while horseshoe magnets bring both poles to the same side for greater lifting force. Demagnetization occurs when atomic alignment is disrupted by heat, physical shock, or improper storage, causing poles to disappear and the object to lose magnetism.
