SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: What is Magnetic Force
DEFINITION:
Magnetic force is a push or pull exerted by a magnet on certain materials or on other magnets, and it is a non-contact force that can act over a distance.

KEY_POINTS:

Magnetic force does not require physical contact with objects.
It operates over varying distances depending on magnet strength.
It can affect both magnetic materials and other magnets.

EXAMPLES:
A magnet can pull a paperclip across a table without touching it.

KEYWORDS:
magnetic force, non-contact force, push, pull, distance force

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: Attractive Force
DEFINITION:
Attractive force is a pulling effect that occurs between a magnet and a magnetic material or between opposite poles of two magnets.

KEY_POINTS:

Attractive force pulls objects toward the magnet.
It occurs between North and South poles.
It occurs between magnets and magnetic materials like iron.

EXAMPLES:
A North Pole magnet pulled toward a South Pole attracts and sticks together.

KEYWORDS:
attractive force, pulling, opposite poles, magnet attraction, iron magnetism

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Repulsive Force
DEFINITION:
Repulsive force is a pushing effect that occurs only between the same poles of two magnets.

KEY_POINTS:

Repulsive force pushes objects away from each other.
It occurs only between similar poles (North-North or South-South).
It produces a cushioning resistance when poles are forced together.

EXAMPLES:
Two North Poles pushed together create a pushing sensation strong enough to prevent contact.

KEYWORDS:
repulsive force, pushing, same poles, magnetic repulsion, cushion effect

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C4
NAME: Effect of Distance on Magnetic Force
DEFINITION:
The strength of magnetic force decreases as distance between the magnet and object increases, with closer proximity producing stronger force.

KEY_POINTS:

Closer magnets exert stronger force on objects.
Distant magnets exert weaker force.
Force eventually becomes too weak to move objects.

EXAMPLES:
A magnet can pick up a pin from 1 centimeter away but may not reach a pin 10 centimeters away.

KEYWORDS:
distance, magnetic strength, force weakening, proximity, force range

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C5
NAME: Magnet Strength
DEFINITION:
Different magnets have different natural force strengths, with larger and higher-quality magnets exerting greater force.

KEY_POINTS:

Some magnets are naturally stronger than others.
Large magnets usually produce stronger force than small ones.
High-quality magnets exert greater pulling or pushing power.

EXAMPLES:
A large neodymium magnet will attract iron more strongly than a small ceramic magnet.

KEYWORDS:
magnet strength, quality, size, force intensity, magnetic power

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C6
NAME: Magnetic Field
DEFINITION:
A magnetic field is the invisible space around a magnet where magnetic force exists and can be visualized as lines of force flowing from North to South.

KEY_POINTS:

Magnetic force exists throughout the space around a magnet.
Iron filings reveal field line patterns arcing from North to South.
Field strength decreases with distance from the magnet.

EXAMPLES:
Iron filings sprinkled around a magnet arrange into curved line patterns showing the invisible field.

KEYWORDS:
magnetic field, field lines, invisible force, iron filings pattern, force visualization

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C7
NAME: Magnetic Force Through Different Media
DEFINITION:
Magnetic force can pass through various non-magnetic materials including solids, liquids, and gases.

KEY_POINTS:

Magnetic force travels through solid materials like wood.
It passes through liquids like water and glass containers.
It moves easily through air and other gases.

EXAMPLES:
Moving a magnet under a wooden table can move a needle on top of the table.

KEYWORDS:
transparency, transmission, solids, liquids, gases, force penetration

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C8
NAME: Material Composition vs Mass in Magnetism
DEFINITION:
Magnetic force depends only on what an object is made of, not how heavy it is; non-magnetic materials experience zero force regardless of size.

KEY_POINTS:

Weight does not determine magnetic attraction.
Material composition is the only factor that matters.
A tiny iron pin attracts while a huge gold block does not.

EXAMPLES:
In Sri Lankan recycling centers, magnets separate iron and steel cans from aluminum cans by composition, not weight.

KEYWORDS:
composition, material type, weight independence, magnetic materials, non-magnetic

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C9
NAME: Induced Magnetism
DEFINITION:
Induced magnetism is the temporary creation of magnetic properties in an object when exposed to a magnetic force.

KEY_POINTS:

Iron becomes a temporary magnet when exposed to a permanent magnet.
Induced magnetism disappears when the source magnet is removed.
The induced pole is always opposite to the source pole.

EXAMPLES:
A nail becomes magnetized when held near a strong magnet and can pick up smaller pins, but loses magnetism when removed.

KEYWORDS:
induced magnetism, temporary magnet, magnetization, demagnetization

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C10
NAME: Overcoming Friction with Magnetic Force
DEFINITION:
Magnetic repulsive force can lift objects above surfaces to eliminate friction, allowing higher speeds and reduced wear.

KEY_POINTS:

Magnetic force can lift trains above tracks.
Eliminating friction allows higher speeds.
Maglev trains move faster because wheels do not rub tracks.

EXAMPLES:
Maglev trains use magnetic repulsion to float centimeters above tracks, reducing friction and allowing faster travel than normal Sri Lankan trains.

KEYWORDS:
friction reduction, levitation, maglev train, frictionless motion, speed increase

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C11
NAME: Dangers of Magnetic Force to Electronics
DEFINITION:
Strong magnetic forces can damage electronic devices by disrupting stored information or harming sensitive components.

KEY_POINTS:

Credit cards have magnetic strips that store information.
Strong magnets can scramble magnetic strip data.
Smartphones and screens can be damaged by strong magnetic fields.

EXAMPLES:
A strong magnet too close to a credit card can jumble information on the magnetic strip, making the card unusable.

KEYWORDS:
magnetic damage, electronic protection, credit card, data loss, device safety

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C12
NAME: Earth's Magnetic Field
DEFINITION:
Earth's magnetic field is a giant protective force that deflects charged particles from the sun and shields the planet from harmful radiation.

KEY_POINTS:

Earth's magnetic field acts as a protective shield.
It deflects solar winds and charged particles.
It prevents dangerous solar radiation from reaching atmosphere.

EXAMPLES:
Without Earth's magnetic field protection, sun radiation would make life very difficult on Earth.

KEYWORDS:
Earth magnetic field, solar wind, radiation protection, planetary shield

SECTION: SUMMARY
Magnetic force is a non-contact force exerted by magnets on magnetic materials and other magnets over a distance. Attractive force pulls unlike poles together, while repulsive force pushes like poles apart. Force strength depends on two factors: distance (closer produces stronger force) and magnet strength (larger quality magnets produce greater force). Magnetic force exists in invisible fields visualized by iron filing patterns flowing from North to South Pole. This force can pass through solids, liquids, and gases. Importantly, magnetic force depends on material composition, not mass, so a tiny iron pin attracts while a large non-magnetic gold block does not. When magnets contact iron objects, induced magnetism creates temporary magnetism that disappears when the source magnet is removed. Maglev trains use magnetic repulsion to levitate above tracks and friction-free motion allows faster speeds than normal trains. Strong magnetic forces can damage electronic devices such as credit cards and smartphones, so magnets must be kept away from sensitive electronics. Finally, Earth's magnetic field acts as a protective shield, deflecting harmful solar winds and radiation to make life possible on our planet.
