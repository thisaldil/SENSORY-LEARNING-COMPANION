SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: Animal Locomotion
DEFINITION:
Locomotion is the ability to move the entire body from one place to another, a characteristic that most animals possess to find food, escape danger, or find shelter.

KEY_POINTS:

Animals can move their whole body from location to location.
Locomotion serves survival functions like finding food and avoiding predators.
Movement requires high energy and quick responses.

EXAMPLES:
A squirrel darts around a tree constantly moving to gather food and escape threats, requiring immediate energy.

KEYWORDS:
locomotion, animal movement, body movement, escape danger, find food, survival

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: Plant Fixed Position and Part Movement
DEFINITION:
Plants are generally fixed to one spot by their roots and do not show locomotion, but they do show movements of parts such as flowers opening or leaves turning toward the sun.

KEY_POINTS:

Plants remain rooted in one location throughout their lives.
Plants do not have locomotion like animals.
Plants show part movements such as leaf orientation and flower opening.

EXAMPLES:
A plant's leaves turn toward the sun to maximize light capture, and flowers open and close in response to time of day.

KEYWORDS:
rooted, fixed position, no locomotion, part movement, leaves, flowers, phototropism

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Animal Nutrition as Consumers
DEFINITION:
Animals are consumers (heterotrophs) that cannot make their own food and must eat plants or other animals to obtain energy.

KEY_POINTS:

Animals cannot produce their own food.
Animals must consume plants or other animals for energy.
Animals are called consumers or heterotrophs.

EXAMPLES:
A squirrel living in a Jak tree must eat the Jak fruit to get energy because it cannot make its own food.

KEYWORDS:
consumers, heterotrophs, eating, animal nutrition, cannot make food, energy intake

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C4
NAME: Plant Nutrition and Photosynthesis
DEFINITION:
Green plants are producers (autotrophs) that make their own food using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide through the process of photosynthesis.

KEY_POINTS:

Plants produce their own food through photosynthesis.
Photosynthesis uses sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide.
Plants are called producers or autotrophs.

EXAMPLES:
A massive Jak tree uses sunlight, water from soil, and carbon dioxide from air to make its own food through photosynthesis.

KEYWORDS:
photosynthesis, producers, autotrophs, make own food, sunlight, water, carbon dioxide, green plants

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C5
NAME: Animal Sensory Organs
DEFINITION:
Animals have well-developed sensory organs like eyes, ears, and nose that allow them to respond quickly to their surroundings.

KEY_POINTS:

Animals possess specialized sensory organs.
These organs include eyes, ears, nose, and other sense receptors.
Sensory organs enable quick responses to environmental changes.

EXAMPLES:
A dog uses its eyes to see danger, ears to hear sounds, and nose to smell food, responding immediately to its environment.

KEYWORDS:
sensory organs, eyes, ears, nose, quick response, detect environment, animal senses

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C6
NAME: Plant Response Without Sense Organs
DEFINITION:
Plants do not have nervous systems or sense organs like animals, but they still respond slowly to environmental factors such as light and gravity.

KEY_POINTS:

Plants lack nervous systems and specialized sense organs.
Plants respond to stimuli like light and gravity.
Plant responses are slow compared to animal responses.

EXAMPLES:
A plant slowly grows toward a window over days, responding to light without eyes or a nervous system.

KEYWORDS:
no nervous system, no sense organs, slow response, light response, gravity response, tropism

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C7
NAME: Energy Strategy Differences
DEFINITION:
Animals are consumers requiring high, immediate energy delivered by complex systems like hearts and lungs, while plants are producers with lower, consistent energy needs met by xylem and phloem transport systems.

KEY_POINTS:

Animals require high amounts of energy quickly for active movement.
Animals have hearts to pump blood and lungs for oxygen intake.
Plants have lower, more consistent energy needs.
Plants use xylem and phloem tubes to transport water and food slowly.

EXAMPLES:
In Sri Lanka, a Jak tree stays in one place for decades slowly building its trunk using xylem and phloem, while a squirrel in that tree constantly darts around requiring immediate fuel from a heart and lungs.

KEYWORDS:
energy strategies, consumers, producers, heart, lungs, xylem, phloem, transport systems, Jak tree, squirrel

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C8
NAME: Growth Pattern Differences
DEFINITION:
Animals have determinate growth that stops at a certain size and age, while plants often have indeterminate growth continuing throughout their lives under the right conditions.

KEY_POINTS:

Animals have determinate growth that stops at maturity.
A human child eventually stops getting taller.
Plants have indeterminate growth continuing with proper conditions.
Plants can grow new branches and leaves throughout their entire lives.
Most animals cannot regrow lost limbs, but plants can replace pruned parts.

EXAMPLES:
You can prune a hibiscus hedge in your garden and it will simply grow new shoots to replace the old ones, demonstrating indeterminate growth.

KEYWORDS:
determinate growth, indeterminate growth, growth patterns, pruning, regrowth, hibiscus, maturity

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C9
NAME: Cellular Structure Differences
DEFINITION:
Animal cells are flexible and soft allowing for movement and different body shapes, while plant cells are encased in a rigid cell wall made of cellulose that provides structural support.

KEY_POINTS:

Animal cells are flexible and soft.
Plant cells have a rigid cell wall made of cellulose.
The cell wall acts like a skeleton for plants, providing strength without bones.
Cell structure explains why wood is hard and animal fur is soft.

EXAMPLES:
Under a compound microscope, plant cells show rigid cell walls giving wood its hardness, while animal cells are flexible giving a dog's fur its softness.

KEYWORDS:
cell wall, cellulose, animal cells, plant cells, flexible, rigid, cellular structure, microscope

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C10
NAME: Respiration and Gas Exchange Balance
DEFINITION:
Both plants and animals respire to produce energy, but they interact with air differently creating a complementary relationship where animals consume oxygen and release carbon dioxide while plants use carbon dioxide and release oxygen during photosynthesis.

KEY_POINTS:

Animals breathe in oxygen and breathe out carbon dioxide constantly.
Plants respire like animals, taking in oxygen and releasing carbon dioxide.
During photosynthesis, plants take in carbon dioxide and release oxygen.
This give-and-take relationship keeps Earth's atmosphere balanced.

EXAMPLES:
Animals breathe out carbon dioxide that plants use for photosynthesis, and plants release oxygen that animals need to breathe, creating a balanced cycle.

KEYWORDS:
respiration, gas exchange, oxygen, carbon dioxide, photosynthesis, atmospheric balance, complementary relationship

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C11
NAME: Defense Mechanisms
DEFINITION:
Animals use fight-or-flight defenses enabled by movement, while plants being immobile have developed physical defenses like thorns and chemical defenses like bitter or poisonous compounds.

KEY_POINTS:

Animals can run away or fight back as primary defenses.
Plants cannot run, so they evolved alternative defenses.
Physical plant defenses include thorns and spines.
Chemical plant defenses include bitter tastes and poisonous compounds.

EXAMPLES:
Roses and bougainvillea plants in Sri Lanka have thorns to deter animals from eating them, while other plants produce bitter or poisonous chemicals for protection.

KEYWORDS:
defense mechanisms, fight or flight, thorns, chemical defense, poisonous, bitter, rose, bougainvillea, protection

SECTION: SUMMARY
Plants and animals are both living organisms that grow, breathe, and reproduce, but they have fundamentally different survival strategies. Animals possess locomotion to move their entire body to find food, escape danger, or find shelter, while plants remain fixed in place by roots showing only part movements like leaves turning toward the sun. Animals are consumers (heterotrophs) that must eat plants or other animals because they cannot make their own food, whereas green plants are producers (autotrophs) that make their own food through photosynthesis using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide. Animals have well-developed sensory organs like eyes, ears, and nose for quick environmental responses, while plants respond slowly to light and gravity without nervous systems or specialized sense organs. The energy strategies differ fundamentally: animals require high immediate energy delivered by hearts pumping blood and lungs exchanging oxygen to support active movement, while plants have lower consistent energy needs met by xylem and phloem tubes slowly transporting water and nutrients—as seen in Sri Lanka where a Jak tree stays in one place for decades while a squirrel constantly requires immediate fuel. Animals have determinate growth stopping at maturity, but plants have indeterminate growth continuing throughout life with proper conditions, allowing a hibiscus hedge to grow new shoots after pruning. At the cellular level, plant cells have rigid cell walls made of cellulose providing structural support like a skeleton, explaining why wood is hard, while animal cells are flexible allowing movement and body shape variation. Both respire to produce energy, but their gas exchange creates a complementary atmospheric balance: animals breathe in oxygen and release carbon dioxide constantly, while plants not only respire but also perform photosynthesis taking in the carbon dioxide animals produce and releasing the oxygen animals need. Finally, defense mechanisms reflect their mobility differences: animals use fight-or-flight responses enabled by locomotion, while immobile plants developed physical defenses like thorns on roses and bougainvillea and chemical defenses producing bitter or poisonous compounds to deter consumption.
