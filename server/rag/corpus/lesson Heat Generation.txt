SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: Heat as a Form of Energy
DEFINITION:
Heat is a form of energy felt as warmth, and it is generated by converting other forms of energy into thermal energy.

KEY_POINTS:

Heat is essential for life and daily activities.
Heat generation always involves energy conversion.
Different methods produce heat in different ways.

EXAMPLES:
A cooking stove converts fuel energy into heat used to cook food.

KEYWORDS:
heat energy, thermal energy, warmth, energy conversion

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: Common Heat Sources in Daily Life
DEFINITION:
Heat can be generated naturally from the Sun and artificially through friction, combustion, and electricity.

KEY_POINTS:

The Sun is the main natural source of heat for Earth.
Friction generates heat when surfaces rub together.
Combustion and electrical appliances are common human-controlled heat sources.

EXAMPLES:
In Sri Lanka, many homes use LP gas or firewood for cooking, while irons and kettles generate heat from electricity.

KEYWORDS:
sun heat, friction heat, combustion, electrical heating, cooking heat

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Molecular View of Heat
DEFINITION:
Heat is related to the motion of atoms and molecules, and increasing heat means particles move or vibrate faster.

KEY_POINTS:

Cold objects have slower particle motion.
Heating increases particle motion and vibration.
Temperature rise reflects increased molecular motion.

EXAMPLES:
Rubbing palms together makes skin molecules move faster, causing a warm feeling.

KEYWORDS:
molecules, atoms, particle motion, temperature, thermal motion

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C4
NAME: Heat from Chemical Reactions
DEFINITION:
Some chemical reactions release heat, especially combustion and biological respiration.

KEY_POINTS:

Combustion occurs when fuels react with oxygen to release heat and often light.
Burning wood, coal, LP gas, and kerosene are combustion examples.
Respiration in the human body releases energy from food and helps maintain body temperature near 37°C.

EXAMPLES:
When firewood burns for cooking, chemical energy in wood changes into heat energy.

KEYWORDS:
chemical reaction, combustion, oxygen, respiration, body temperature

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C5
NAME: Frictional Heat in Practical Uses
DEFINITION:
Frictional heat is generated when moving surfaces resist each other and convert mechanical energy into thermal energy.

KEY_POINTS:

Friction was used historically to start fire.
Modern brakes also generate significant frictional heat.
Higher rubbing force or speed usually increases heat generation.

EXAMPLES:
When a bus descends hills in Kandy and brakes repeatedly, brake parts become very hot due to friction.

KEYWORDS:
friction, mechanical energy, brake heat, fire drill, thermal conversion

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C6
NAME: Electrical Heating and Resistance
DEFINITION:
Electrical heating happens when electric current passes through a high-resistance material and energy is converted to heat.

KEY_POINTS:

Heating elements in appliances are often made of nichrome.
Electrical resistance causes collisions that produce heat.
This method avoids smoke at the point of heating compared with direct fuel burning.

EXAMPLES:
A toaster element heats and glows because current passing through nichrome generates thermal energy.

KEYWORDS:
electrical heating, resistance, nichrome, heating element, current

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C7
NAME: Heat from Gas Compression
DEFINITION:
Compressing a gas can raise its temperature because molecules are forced into a smaller volume and collide more frequently.

KEY_POINTS:

Gas compression increases molecular collisions and thermal energy.
Bicycle pumps can feel warm after repeated use.
Diesel engines use compression heating to help ignite fuel.

EXAMPLES:
After inflating a tire quickly, the lower part of a bicycle pump becomes noticeably warm.

KEYWORDS:
gas compression, pressure heating, bicycle pump, diesel ignition, collisions

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C8
NAME: Geothermal Heat and Sri Lankan Hot Springs
DEFINITION:
Geothermal heat is Earth’s internal heat from planetary formation and radioactive processes, which can warm underground water.

KEY_POINTS:

Earth stores large amounts of internal heat.
In some places geothermal heat appears as hot springs or volcanic activity.
Sri Lanka has hot springs such as Mahapelessa and Kanniya, even without active volcanoes.

EXAMPLES:
Natural warm water at Kanniya hot springs is heated by Earth’s internal geothermal energy.

KEYWORDS:
geothermal energy, internal Earth heat, hot springs, Mahapelessa, Kanniya

SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M1
STATEMENT:
Heat is a material stored inside objects, not a form of energy.

CORRECTION:
Heat is energy transfer associated with particle motion and temperature differences, not a substance.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M2
STATEMENT:
Only fire can generate heat.

CORRECTION:
Heat can also be generated by sunlight, friction, electrical resistance, gas compression, and geothermal processes.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M3

CORRECTION:
Electrical heating in appliances depends on resistance in materials such as nichrome, where collisions convert electrical energy to heat.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M4

CORRECTION:
Rapid compression usually raises air temperature because molecular collisions increase in a smaller volume.

SECTION: SUMMARY
Heat is a form of energy generated when other energy forms are converted into thermal energy. Common sources include the Sun, friction, combustion, and electricity, and each method can be explained by increased particle motion at the molecular level. Chemical reactions such as burning and respiration release heat, while friction, resistance heating, and gas compression are important practical processes seen in daily life and technology. Geothermal heat shows that Earth itself is also a heat source, as seen in Sri Lankan hot springs at Mahapelessa and Kanniya. Understanding these processes helps students connect science to real life and use heat sources safely.
