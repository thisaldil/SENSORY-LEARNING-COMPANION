SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: Climate Change and Extreme Weather
DEFINITION:
Climate change increases Earth’s heat, making weather events more extreme, more frequent, and often more destructive.

KEY_POINTS:

Warmer climate adds extra energy to the atmosphere.
Events that were rare in the past now happen more often.
Many climate-related disasters become stronger than before.

EXAMPLES:
Heavy rain events and long dry periods now occur more often in many regions compared with decades ago.

KEYWORDS:
climate change, extreme weather, warming, atmospheric energy, disaster frequency

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: Floods and Droughts in the Hydrological Cycle
DEFINITION:
Floods and droughts are opposite climate-related disasters caused by imbalance in rainfall, evaporation, and water storage.

KEY_POINTS:

Floods occur when rainfall is so heavy that soil and drains cannot absorb or carry water.
Droughts occur when rainfall is absent for long periods, drying soil and water sources.
Warming increases both intense rainfall and rapid evaporation, making wet places wetter and dry places drier.

EXAMPLES:
In Sri Lanka, monsoon floods are common in the Low Country, while droughts can dry wewas and damage crops.

KEYWORDS:
flood, drought, rainfall, evaporation, monsoon, wewa, hydrological cycle

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Landslides, Saturated Soil, and Deforestation
DEFINITION:
Landslides occur when water-saturated slope soil loses stability and moves downhill, especially where vegetation cover has been removed.

KEY_POINTS:

Heavy rain fills soil pores and increases soil weight.
Water acts like a lubricant, reducing friction between soil layers.
Tree roots hold soil together, so deforestation raises landslide risk.

EXAMPLES:
Hill Country areas such as Badulla and Kegalle face high landslide danger after prolonged heavy rain, especially on cleared slopes.

KEYWORDS:
landslide, saturated soil, deforestation, slope stability, gravity, Badulla, Kegalle

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C4
NAME: Cyclones and Indian Ocean Warming
DEFINITION:
Cyclones are rotating storm systems that gain energy from warm ocean water, producing strong winds and very heavy rain.

KEY_POINTS:

Cyclones behave like heat engines powered by warm sea surfaces.
Warmer Indian Ocean water gives cyclones more energy and moisture.
Sri Lankan authorities track low-pressure systems and issue warning levels for safety.

EXAMPLES:
When the Department of Meteorology detects a low-pressure area strengthening, Yellow, Orange, and Red warnings guide evacuation decisions.

KEYWORDS:
cyclone, Indian Ocean, ocean warming, low pressure, meteorology warnings, evacuation

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C5
NAME: Sea Level Rise, Coastal Erosion, and Mangroves
DEFINITION:
Sea level rise from ice melt and thermal expansion increases coastal erosion and flood damage, but natural and engineered barriers reduce impacts.

KEY_POINTS:

Rising temperature melts polar ice and expands seawater volume.
Higher sea levels push waves farther inland during tides and storms.
Breakwaters and mangrove roots absorb wave energy and protect coastal land.

EXAMPLES:
Sri Lankan coastal communities use breakwaters and mangrove planting to reduce beach loss and home damage.

KEYWORDS:
sea level rise, thermal expansion, coastal erosion, breakwater, mangrove, coastal protection

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C6
NAME: El Niño, La Niña, and Seasonal Forecasting
DEFINITION:
El Niño and La Niña are climate cycles linked to Pacific Ocean temperatures that influence rainfall patterns in Sri Lanka.

KEY_POINTS:

El Niño often brings drier conditions and drought risk to the region.
La Niña often increases rainfall and flood risk.
Forecasts help farmers choose crops and water plans before hazards intensify.

EXAMPLES:
If drought is predicted, farmers may switch to lower-water crops such as mung beans or maize.

KEYWORDS:
El Niño, La Niña, climate cycle, seasonal forecast, agriculture planning, drought warning

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C7
NAME: Disaster Preparedness and Risk Reduction
DEFINITION:
Preparedness reduces disaster damage through early warning response, emergency planning, and environmental management.

KEY_POINTS:

People must follow Disaster Management Center alerts and official weather news.
Families in risk zones should maintain emergency kits with essential supplies and documents.
Keeping drains clear and reducing plastic waste lowers urban flood risk.

EXAMPLES:
A family in a flood-prone area keeps a torch, radio, first-aid kit, and documents ready to evacuate quickly.

KEYWORDS:
preparedness, DMC alerts, emergency kit, evacuation, drainage, plastic waste, risk reduction

SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M1
STATEMENT:
Climate change only causes hotter days and does not affect disasters.

CORRECTION:
Climate change increases atmospheric and ocean energy, which can intensify floods, droughts, cyclones, and other hazards.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M2
STATEMENT:
Landslides are caused only by rain, so tree cutting does not matter.

CORRECTION:
Deforestation removes roots that bind soil, so rainfall triggers landslides more easily on cleared slopes.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M3
STATEMENT:

CORRECTION:
Early warnings are critical because they give time for preparation, evacuation, and life-saving actions.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M4
STATEMENT:
Mangroves are only plants and do not protect people.

CORRECTION:
Mangroves are natural coastal barriers; their roots absorb wave energy and reduce erosion and flood damage.

SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M5

CORRECTION:

SECTION: SUMMARY
Climate change increases atmospheric and ocean heat, which strengthens many natural hazards and makes them more frequent. Warmer air holds more water vapor, causing heavier rain and floods, while faster evaporation can worsen droughts and dry out soils and wewas. In hill regions such as Badulla and Kegalle, heavy rain combined with deforestation increases landslide risk because saturated soil loses stability without root support. Cyclones over the Indian Ocean gain energy from warm seawater, so monitoring and color-coded warnings from meteorological services are essential for safe evacuation. Rising sea levels from ice melt and thermal expansion increase coastal erosion, and protection measures such as breakwaters and mangrove planting reduce wave damage. Climate cycles such as El Niño and La Niña help forecast drought or flood tendencies, allowing better crop planning. Disaster risk can be reduced through preparedness: following DMC alerts, maintaining emergency kits, keeping drains clear, and protecting ecosystems.
