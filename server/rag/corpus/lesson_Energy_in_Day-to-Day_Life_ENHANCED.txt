SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: Continuous Energy Use in Living
DEFINITION:
Every activity performed by living organisms, from breathing and thinking to moving and growing, requires energy in various forms operating simultaneously.

KEY_POINTS:

All life activities consume energy continuously, even at rest.
Multiple energy types work together in the body simultaneously.
Brain uses electrical energy, body burns chemical energy from food, eyes receive light energy.
Even reading requires energy expenditure in multiple body systems.

EXAMPLES:
While reading a book, your brain uses electrical energy for thinking, your body burns chemical energy from food to maintain cells, light energy enters your eyes to see, and your heart uses mechanical energy to pump blood.

KEYWORDS:
continuous energy use, life activities, multiple energy forms, simultaneous processes, body energy

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: Sunlight Energy Transfer to Eyes
DEFINITION:
Sunlight is electromagnetic energy that travels 150 million kilometers from the Sun to Earth at the speed of light, entering our eyes and being converted to electrical signals in the brain.

KEY_POINTS:

Sunlight travels vast distances at light speed to reach Earth.
Light energy enters the eye and stimulates photoreceptor cells.
The eye converts light energy into electrical nerve signals.
Brain interprets these signals as vision.

EXAMPLES:
At 6:00 AM, sunlight travels from the Sun through your window, enters your eyes, and sends electrical signals to your brain allowing you to see the new day.

KEYWORDS:
sunlight, light energy, electromagnetic radiation, vision, eye, photoreceptors, speed of light

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Food as Chemical Energy Storage
DEFINITION:
Food contains chemical energy stored in molecular bonds of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats, with different foods providing different amounts and types of energy measured in calories.

KEY_POINTS:

Chemical energy is stored in food molecules.
Carbohydrates provide long-lasting energy.
Proteins build muscles and provide energy.
Fats are energy-dense providing concentrated calories.
One calorie is a unit measuring energy content.

EXAMPLES:
Breakfast foods provide varied energy: cereal and bread give long-lasting carbohydrate energy, eggs provide muscle-building protein energy, butter provides energy-dense fat, and rice or roti provide sustained morning energy.

KEYWORDS:
chemical energy, food energy, calories, carbohydrates, proteins, fats, energy storage, molecular bonds

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C4
NAME: Digestion and Energy Release Process
DEFINITION:
Digestion is the chemical and mechanical breakdown of food in the digestive system that releases stored chemical energy for absorption and use by body cells.

KEY_POINTS:

Chewing provides mechanical breakdown of food.
Stomach performs chemical digestion through acids and enzymes.
Chemical reactions release energy from food molecules.
Energy is absorbed by intestines into bloodstream.
Released energy powers all body functions.

EXAMPLES:
When you eat a banana containing approximately 100 calories of chemical energy, your digestive system breaks it down through chewing and stomach acids, releases the energy, and your body absorbs it providing enough power to run around for 15 minutes.

KEYWORDS:
digestion, chemical breakdown, mechanical breakdown, energy release, absorption, stomach, enzymes, chemical reactions

SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C5
NAME: Electrical Energy Transformations in Devices
DEFINITION:
Electrical energy from power plants travels through wires to homes and transforms into other energy forms such as light, heat, and mechanical energy in various household devices.

KEY_POINTS:

Power plants generate electrical energy transmitted through wires.
Electrical energy transforms into multiple useful forms in devices.
Light bulbs convert electrical to light and heat energy.
Water heaters convert electrical to heat energy.
Fans and hair dryers convert electrical to mechanical energy.

EXAMPLES:
In your bathroom, a light bulb converts electrical energy to light and heat so you can see yourself, a water heater converts electrical energy to heat for warm showers, and a hair dryer converts electrical energy to heat and mechanical energy (blowing air) to dry your hair.

KEYWORDS:
electrical energy, energy transformation, light bulbs, appliances, heat conversion, mechanical conversion, power plants, wires

CONCEPT_ID: C6
NAME: Muscle Movement and Mechanical Energy
DEFINITION:
Muscles convert chemical energy from food into mechanical energy for movement by contracting and relaxing, enabling locomotion and physical work.

KEY_POINTS:

Food energy is delivered to muscle cells.
Muscle contraction requires chemical energy.
Mechanical energy results from muscle movement.
Physical work burns calories from food stores.

EXAMPLES:
Walking to school burns approximately 100 calories while cycling burns approximately 200 calories because cycling requires more mechanical work from your muscles converting food energy into movement.

KEYWORDS:
mechanical energy, muscle contraction, locomotion, physical work, calorie burning, movement, muscle cells

School activities involve multiple simultaneous energy transformations including electrical energy for lighting and computers, sound energy for communication, and chemical energy for thinking and learning.

Classroom lighting converts electrical to light energy (60 watts per bulb).
Sound waves carry energy from teachers to students' ears.
Computers use electrical energy processed into light displays.
Brain learning consumes chemical energy from food.
Mechanical energy from fingers operates keyboards and mice.

During computer class, electrical energy powers the computer circuits and screen light, mechanical energy from your fingers operates the keyboard and mouse, and your brain uses chemical energy to process information and learn.

CONCEPT_ID: C8
NAME: Physical Activity Energy Expenditure
DEFINITION:
Sports and physical activities consume large amounts of chemical energy from food, converting it to mechanical movement and heat, with intensity determining calorie burn rate.

KEY_POINTS:

Physical activity burns chemical energy at high rates.
Energy converts to mechanical movement and body heat.
30 minutes of running burns 300+ calories.
Intense activity requires additional food energy intake.
Mechanical energy transfers to objects like balls during sports.

EXAMPLES:
Kicking a football transfers your leg's mechanical energy to the ball creating kinetic energy in motion, the impact creates sound energy, and you feel tired because you've burned lots of chemical energy during 30 minutes of play.

NAME: Digital Device Energy Consumption
DEFINITION:
Electronic devices like phones, tablets, and game consoles consume electrical energy from batteries or outlets, converting it primarily to light from screens and heat from processors.

Devices store electrical energy in batteries or draw from outlets.
Screens emit light energy from pixels.
Processors generate heat as byproduct.
Game consoles use 100-200 watts of power.
Device settings affect energy consumption rates.

EXAMPLES:
When gaming, your phone's battery provides electrical energy to circuits, the screen converts it to light energy for display, and your brain uses energy thinking and reacting, while leaving devices charged unnecessarily wastes energy.

CONCEPT_ID: C10
NAME: Cooking Heat Energy Transformation
DEFINITION:
Cooking transforms chemical energy from fuels (wood, gas, electricity) into heat energy that causes chemical changes in food, making it edible and releasing nutrients for digestion.

KEY_POINTS:

Fuel sources provide chemical or electrical energy.
Energy converts to heat for cooking.
Heat causes chemical changes making food edible.
Different cooking methods have different energy efficiency.
Cooked food energy becomes available to body after eating.

EXAMPLES:
In Sri Lankan traditional cooking, firewood contains chemical energy that burns to produce heat energy, which cooks rice through chemical changes, and when eaten the rice energy is absorbed by the family providing power for their activities.

SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C11
NAME: Sleep Energy Recovery and Storage
DEFINITION:
During sleep, the body continues using energy for digestion, repair, growth, and memory consolidation while storing excess energy for future use, making sleep essential for energy management.

KEY_POINTS:

Sleep is energy-intensive despite appearing restful.
Body performs digestion, repair, and growth during sleep.
Energy stores as fat and glucose for next day.
Brain consolidates memories using energy.
Children need 8-10 hours for growth processes.

EXAMPLES:
After burning 2000-2500 calories throughout the day, an 11-year-old sleeps through the night while the body repairs muscles using protein, stores excess energy, brain consolidates learning, and healing processes occur, waking up refreshed and recharged.

KEYWORDS:
sleep, energy storage, body repair, growth, memory consolidation, glucose storage, fat storage, recovery, energy management

SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C12
NAME: Daily Energy Budget and Balance
DEFINITION:
A daily energy budget balances energy intake from food (calories consumed) against energy expenditure from all activities (calories burned), with excess stored or deficit requiring stored energy use.

KEY_POINTS:

11-year-olds typically burn 1700-2400 calories daily.
Daily needs are 2000-2500 calories to balance energy.
Energy in equals energy out plus storage.
Different activities burn calories at different rates.
Sleep burns 400-500 calories, school burns 600-800 calories, sports burn 400-600 calories.

EXAMPLES:
If breakfast provides 400 calories and walking to school burns 100 calories while cycling burns 200 calories, you still have 200-300 calories remaining for school activities, demonstrating energy budget management.

KEYWORDS:
energy budget, calorie balance, energy intake, energy expenditure, daily needs, calorie counting, energy equation, metabolism

SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C13
NAME: Energy Efficiency and Conservation
DEFINITION:
Energy efficiency means using less energy to accomplish the same task, achieved through technology choices and behavior changes that reduce waste and environmental impact.

KEY_POINTS:

LED bulbs use 75% less energy than incandescent bulbs.
Turning off unused devices saves electrical energy.
Shorter showers reduce water heating energy.
Cycling instead of driving saves fuel energy.
Individual actions multiplied across populations create massive impact.

EXAMPLES:
Using LED bulbs (10 watts per hour) instead of incandescent bulbs (60 watts per hour) for lighting reduces electricity consumption by 6 times, saving both energy costs and environmental resources.

KEYWORDS:
energy efficiency, conservation, LED bulbs, energy saving, waste reduction, environmental impact, sustainable practices, behavior change

NAME: Global Energy Consumption Patterns
DEFINITION:
Energy consumption per person varies dramatically across countries, with developed nations using far more energy than sustainable levels while global goals aim for renewable energy transition.

Average USA person uses approximately 12,000 watts continuously.
Average Sri Lankan person uses approximately 1,000 watts.
Sustainable target is approximately 2,000 watts per person.
80% of global energy currently from fossil fuels (non-renewable).
20% from renewable sources (solar, wind, hydro).
Goal is 80% renewable energy by 2050.

EXAMPLES:
Sri Lanka's per-person energy consumption of approximately 1,000 watts is far below USA's 12,000 watts, demonstrating that energy use varies by development level and lifestyle, with global sustainability requiring movement toward 2,000 watts per person from renewable sources.

KEYWORDS:
global energy, per capita consumption, fossil fuels, renewable energy, sustainability, energy transition, climate goals, Sri Lanka energy use
