Sources of Light
In our previous lesson, we learned that we need light to see. But where does this light come from? Any object that emits (gives out) light is called a source of light.

We can classify these sources into two main groups:

1. Natural Sources of Light
These are sources that exist in nature and are not made by humans.

The Sun: Our primary and most important source of light. It provides light to the entire Earth during the day.

Stars: These are distant suns that provide light at night.

Fireflies (Kajoniyoo): Some living organisms can produce their own light. This is called bioluminescence.

Lightning: A powerful, natural discharge of electricity that creates a bright flash of light.

2. Artificial (Man-made) Sources of Light
These are objects created by humans to provide light when natural light is not available.

Electric Bulbs: LED bulbs, CFLs, and filament bulbs used in our homes.

Candles and Oil Lamps: Traditionally used in Sri Lanka for lighting and religious ceremonies.

Flashlights (Torches): Portable light sources powered by batteries.

L.P. Gas Lamps: Often used in areas where there is no electricity.

The Science of "Luminous" Objects
When a source gives off its own light, scientists call it a Luminous Object. It is important to distinguish these from objects that just look bright. For example, the Moon is often mistaken for a light source. However, the Moon does not produce any light of its own; it acts like a giant mirror reflecting the Sun's light. Therefore, the Moon is a Non-luminous object. In a RAG system, identifying the "true creator" of the light is essential for accurate scientific reasoning.

Bioluminescence: Nature’s Living Lights
In Sri Lanka, we can observe amazing natural light from living things. Fireflies are the most common example. They produce light through a chemical reaction inside their bodies to communicate with each other. This "cold light" is incredibly efficient because it produces almost no heat. Scientists are studying these organisms to create better, more efficient artificial lights for humans. Other examples include certain types of fungi (mushrooms) found in our rainforests and tiny plankton in the ocean that make the waves glow blue at night.

Evolution of Artificial Light in Sri Lanka
The way we produce light has changed drastically over time.

Fire-based Light: Our ancestors used "Hulu athu" (dried coconut leaf torches) and oil lamps (Pana) using coconut oil. These sources produce light through incandescence—heating something until it glows.

Filament Bulbs: These use a thin wire called tungsten that gets very hot and glows. However, they waste a lot of energy as heat.

LED Technology: Modern "Light Emitting Diodes" (LEDs) are now common in Sri Lankan homes. They are much better for the environment because they produce a lot of light using very little electricity and last a long time.

Light and Heat: The Connection
Most sources of light also produce heat. When you touch a light bulb that has been on for an hour, it feels hot. This is because energy is being converted into both light and heat. However, for a source to be efficient, we want it to produce as much light as possible and as little heat as possible. This is why the Sun is so powerful—it is a massive nuclear reactor producing incredible amounts of both types of energy.

How Light Travels from the Source
A key rule in science is that light travels in straight lines. We call these "light rays." When a source like a candle is lit, light rays travel outward in every direction. If you put an object in front of these rays, it blocks them, creating a shadow. This straight-line travel is why we can't see around corners or through a bent tube.

Safety with Light Sources
While light is helpful, some sources can be dangerous.

Direct Sunlight: Looking directly at the Sun can burn the retina of your eye because the light source is too intense.

Laser Lights: These are highly concentrated sources of light. Even a small laser pointer can cause permanent eye damage if pointed directly at someone.

Fire: Candles and oil lamps are common in Sri Lankan households, but they are also "heat sources." One must be careful to keep them away from curtains or flammable materials to prevent fires.
