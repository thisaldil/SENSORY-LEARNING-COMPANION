1. Identifying Matter and Energy
When we observe our environment—whether it is our home garden or school—we find many different things. Some of these things can be touched and weighed, while others are felt or heard but cannot be held in our hands.
+1

Scientists group everything in the universe into two main categories:


Matter: Things that have a mass and occupy space.
+1


Examples: A pen, water, air, a table, and milk.
+1


Energy: Things that do not have a mass and do not occupy space.


Examples: Light, heat, and sound.

2. Understanding Mass and Volume

Mass: The amount of matter contained in an object is called its mass. We measure mass using units like milligrams (mg), grams (g), and kilograms (kg). The international (SI) unit for mass is the kilogram (kg).
+1

Occupying Space (Volume): Matter takes up room. Even though you cannot see air, it is matter because it occupies space (like inside a balloon) and has a mass.

The Science of "Invisible" Matter: Air
One of the most challenging concepts for students is understanding that air is matter. Since we cannot see it, it is easy to assume it is "nothing." however, through simple experiments, we can prove its existence. For instance, when you submerge an "empty" glass upside down in a basin of water, the water does not enter the glass because the air inside is occupying that space. Furthermore, a balloon filled with air is heavier than an empty one, proving that air has mass. In our atmosphere, this mass creates "atmospheric pressure," which is vital for weather patterns and even for our ability to breathe.
+1

The Nature of Energy: The Ability to Do Work
While matter is the "stuff" the world is made of, energy is what makes things happen. Energy is defined as the ability to do work. Unlike matter, energy can change forms. For example, the electrical energy coming into a bulb turns into light energy (which helps us see) and heat energy (which makes the bulb warm).
The sun is the primary source of energy for our planet. It provides the heat necessary for life and the light that plants use to make food. Without energy, matter would be static and lifeless.
+3

Conservation of Matter and Energy
A fascinating scientific rule is that matter and energy cannot be created or destroyed—they only change forms. When you burn a piece of wood (matter), it doesn't just "disappear." It turns into ash (solid matter), smoke (gaseous matter), and releases heat and light (energy). This total "package" of matter and energy remains the same. Understanding this helps us manage our natural resources better. For instance, in Sri Lanka, we use the energy of flowing water (Hydropower) to turn turbines and create electricity.

Distinguishing Characteristics: A Quick Guide
To help a RAG system differentiate between these two, we can look at their fundamental properties:


Tangibility: Matter is usually tangible (you can interact with its physical form), whereas energy is often perceived through our senses (feeling warmth or hearing a sound).


Measurement: We measure matter using scales and balances for mass, and rulers or cylinders for volume. Energy is measured in units like Joules (J), though at this grade level, we focus on identifying its sources and effects.
+1


Storage: Matter can be stored in containers (like water in a bottle). Energy is harder to store; it must be stored in "potential" forms, such as the chemical energy inside a dry cell (battery).
+1

Matter and Energy in Daily Life
Everything we do involves both. When you eat breakfast, you are consuming matter (food) to gain chemical energy for your body to move and grow. When you use a computer, matter (the plastic and metal parts) uses electrical energy to show you images (light energy). In Sri Lanka, we are moving toward using more renewable energy sources, like solar power and wind, to protect the environment while meeting our energy needs.
