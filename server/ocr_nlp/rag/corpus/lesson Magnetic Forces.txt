7.5 Magnetic Forces
A magnetic force is the push or pull exerted by a magnet on certain materials or on other magnets. Unlike many forces we experience, like kicking a ball or lifting a bag, magnetic force is a non-contact force. This means it can act on an object even from a distance without touching it.
+2

1. Types of Magnetic Force
Attractive Force: This is a pulling force. It occurs between a magnet and a magnetic material (like iron) or between the opposite poles of two magnets (North and South).

Repulsive Force: This is a pushing force. It occurs only between the same poles of two magnets (North and North or South and South).

2. Factors Affecting the Strength of the Force
Distance: The closer a magnet is to an object, the stronger the magnetic force. As you move the magnet further away, the force weakens until it can no longer move the object.
+1

Magnet Strength: Some magnets are naturally stronger than others. A large, high-quality magnet will exert a greater force than a small, weak one.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The Science of the "Invisible Field"
Magnetic force does not just exist "on" the magnet; it exists in the space around it. This space is called the Magnetic Field. We can visualize this force by using iron filings. If you place a piece of paper over a magnet and sprinkle iron filings on top, the filings will dance and settle into curved lines. These lines show the path of the magnetic force traveling from the North Pole to the South Pole.
+1

Magnetism and Friction
One of the most powerful applications of magnetic force is its ability to overcome friction. In a normal train, the wheels rub against the tracks, which creates friction and slows the train down. However, Maglev (Magnetic Levitation) trains use powerful magnetic repulsive forces to lift the train a few centimeters above the track. Because the train is "floating" on a cushion of magnetic force, there is no friction from wheels, allowing the train to reach incredibly high speeds.

Magnetic Force through Different Media
Magnetic force is quite "stubborn"—it can travel through many materials that are not magnetic.

Solids: You can move a needle on top of a wooden table by moving a magnet underneath it.

Liquids: If you drop a paperclip into a glass of water, you can "fish" it out by sliding a magnet down the outside of the glass. The force passes through the water and the glass.
+1

Gases: Magnetic force travels through the air easily, which is why your fridge door "snaps" shut when it gets close to the frame.

The Difference Between Mass and Magnetic Pull
It is a common mistake to think that heavier objects are always more attracted to magnets. Magnetic force does not care about how much an object weighs; it only cares about what the object is made of. A giant block of gold (which is non-magnetic) will experience zero magnetic force, while a tiny iron pin will be pulled instantly. In Sri Lanka, this property is used in recycling centers to separate iron and steel cans from aluminum ones.

Temporary vs. Permanent Force
When a magnetic force acts on a piece of iron, it actually turns that iron into a magnet for a short time. This is called Induced Magnetism. If you hold a strong magnet near a nail, the nail becomes "magnetized" by the force and can pick up other smaller pins. However, for most materials, as soon as the main magnet is moved away, the magnetic force inside the nail disappears.

Protecting Objects from Magnetic Force
While magnetic force is useful, it can also be harmful to certain items. Many modern gadgets like smartphones, credit cards, and old television screens can be damaged by strong magnetic forces. Credit cards have a "magnetic strip" that stores your information; if a strong magnet gets too close, the force can jumble the information on the strip, making the card useless. This is why we keep magnets away from electronic devices.
+1

The Earth's Protective Force
The largest magnetic force we experience comes from the Earth itself. The Earth’s magnetic field acts like a giant shield. It exerts a force on "Solar Winds" (charged particles from the sun), pushing them away from our atmosphere. Without this magnetic force protecting us, the sun's radiation would make life on Earth very difficult.
