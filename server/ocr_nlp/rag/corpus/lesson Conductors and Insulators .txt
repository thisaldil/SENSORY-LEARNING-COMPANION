Conductors and Insulators
Not all materials behave the same way when they come into contact with electricity. Depending on their internal structure, materials either allow electricity to flow through them or block it entirely.

1. Conductors
Materials that allow electricity to flow through them easily are called Conductors. Most metals are excellent conductors. In a circuit, we use conductors to carry energy from the battery to the bulb.

Examples: Copper, Aluminum, Iron, Gold, and Silver.

Special Case: Graphite (the "lead" in your pencil) is a non-metal that can conduct electricity.

2. Insulators
Materials that do not allow electricity to flow through them are called Insulators. These materials are used to protect us from electric shocks by covering conductive parts.

Examples: Rubber, Plastic, Wood, Glass, and Dry Air.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The Atomic Secret: Why do they conduct?
To understand why a piece of copper conducts but a piece of wood does not, we have to look at the atoms. In metals, some electrons (tiny charged particles) are not tied to any single atom; they are "free" to move around. When you connect a battery, these free electrons start flowing in one direction, creating an electric current. In insulators, the electrons are tightly bound to their atoms and cannot move freely, so no current can flow.

Water: A Dangerous Conductor
A very important lesson for safety in Sri Lanka is that impure water conducts electricity. While pure distilled water is a poor conductor, the water we find in our taps, wells, and rivers contains dissolved minerals and salts. These impurities allow electricity to pass through. This is why you must never touch electrical switches with wet hands. If your hands are wet, the electricity can travel through the water on your skin and into your body, causing a dangerous electric shock.

Electricity and the Human Body
Did you know that the human body is a conductor? About 60–70% of our body is made of water containing salts. This is why electricity is so dangerous to us. If you touch a "live" wire, your body becomes part of the circuit, allowing the electricity to flow through you to the ground. This can stop the heart or cause severe burns. This is why safety equipment, like the rubber handles on an electrician's pliers, is so important.

Applications in Everyday Life
We use the combination of conductors and insulators in almost every electrical device:

Electrical Wires: The inside is made of copper (conductor) to carry the power, while the outside is coated in PVC plastic (insulator) so we can touch the wire safely.

Plug Tops: The pins that go into the wall are made of brass (conductor), but the part you hold is made of hard plastic (insulator).

Power Lines: You may have noticed large ceramic or glass "discs" holding the high-voltage wires on concrete poles. These are Insulators that prevent the electricity from traveling down the pole into the ground.

Testing Materials: The Conductivity Tester
You can easily test if an object is a conductor or an insulator by building a simple "Tester Circuit." Create a circuit with a battery and a bulb, but leave a gap between two wires. If you place a metal key in the gap and the bulb lights up, the key is a conductor. If you place a plastic ruler in the gap and the bulb stays dark, the ruler is an insulator.

Superconductors: The Future of Science
Some materials, when cooled to extremely low temperatures, become Superconductors. This means they have zero resistance, and electricity can flow through them forever without losing any energy as heat. While we don't use these in our homes yet, they are used in giant magnets for MRI machines in hospitals and for high-speed Maglev trains.

Resistance: The "Traffic Jam" for Electrons
Even among conductors, some are better than others. Copper is used for household wiring because it has low resistance (it is easy for electrons to move). Tungsten, a metal used in old light bulbs, has high resistance. As electricity tries to force its way through the thin tungsten wire, it generates a lot of heat and light. This shows that even "conductive" materials can be used to control or change the energy of electricity.
