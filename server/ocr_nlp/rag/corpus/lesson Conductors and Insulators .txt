SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CI_01
NAME: Conductor
DEFINITION:
A conductor is a material that allows electricity to flow through it easily.

KEY_POINTS:
- Most metals are good conductors.
- Conductors are used to carry electric current.
- Electricity flows through conductors because they have free electrons.

EXAMPLES:
Copper
Aluminum
Iron
Gold
Silver
Graphite (non-metal conductor)

KEYWORDS:
electric current, metal, free electrons, flow

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CI_02
NAME: Insulator
DEFINITION:
An insulator is a material that does not allow electricity to flow through it.

KEY_POINTS:
- Insulators block electric current.
- They protect us from electric shock.
- Electrons in insulators are tightly bound to atoms.

EXAMPLES:
Rubber
Plastic
Wood
Glass
Dry air

KEYWORDS:
block electricity, protection, safety

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CI_03
NAME: Atomic Structure and Conductivity
DEFINITION:
The ability of a material to conduct electricity depends on how its electrons are arranged.

KEY_POINTS:
- Metals have free-moving electrons.
- Free electrons move when connected to a battery.
- Insulators have tightly bound electrons.
- Electric current is the movement of electrons.

KEYWORDS:
atoms, electrons, current, structure

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CI_04
NAME: Water and Electricity
DEFINITION:
Impure water can conduct electricity because it contains dissolved salts and minerals.

KEY_POINTS:
- Pure distilled water is a poor conductor.
- Tap water contains minerals.
- Wet hands increase risk of electric shock.
- Water on skin allows electricity to travel into the body.

EXAMPLES:
Touching a switch with wet hands is dangerous.
Flooded areas increase electric shock risk.

KEYWORDS:
impurities, dissolved salts, safety

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CI_05
NAME: Human Body as a Conductor
DEFINITION:
The human body conducts electricity because it contains water and dissolved salts.

KEY_POINTS:
- The body is about 60–70% water.
- Electricity can pass through the body.
- Electric shock can damage organs and stop the heart.
- Safety equipment uses insulating materials.

EXAMPLES:
Rubber handles on tools.
Wearing rubber gloves during repairs.

KEYWORDS:
electric shock, safety, circuit, grounding

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CI_06
NAME: Electrical Resistance
DEFINITION:
Resistance is the opposition to the flow of electric current.

KEY_POINTS:
- Low resistance allows easier flow.
- High resistance produces heat.
- Copper has low resistance.
- Tungsten has high resistance.

EXAMPLES:
Copper wires in homes.
Tungsten filament in bulbs producing light and heat.

KEYWORDS:
resistance, heat, tungsten, copper

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CI_07
NAME: Superconductors
DEFINITION:
Superconductors are materials that have zero resistance at very low temperatures.

KEY_POINTS:
- Electricity flows without energy loss.
- Used in MRI machines.
- Used in Maglev trains.

KEYWORDS:
zero resistance, low temperature, advanced technology

==================================================
SECTION: RELATION
RELATION_ID: REL_CI_01
FROM: Atomic Structure
TO: Conductivity
EXPLANATION:
Materials conduct electricity if they have free electrons that can move.

==================================================
SECTION: RELATION
RELATION_ID: REL_CI_02
FROM: Water
TO: Electric Shock
EXPLANATION:
Water containing dissolved salts allows electricity to pass through the human body.

==================================================
SECTION: RELATION
RELATION_ID: REL_CI_03
FROM: Resistance
TO: Heat Production
EXPLANATION:
High resistance converts electrical energy into heat and light.

==================================================
SECTION: EXAMPLE
EXAMPLE_ID: EX_CI_01
TITLE: Electrical Wire

DESCRIPTION:
A wire has copper inside to carry electricity. The outside is covered with plastic to prevent electric shock.

==================================================
SECTION: EXAMPLE
EXAMPLE_ID: EX_CI_02
TITLE: Conductivity Tester

DESCRIPTION:
A simple circuit with a battery and bulb can test materials. If the bulb lights when a material is placed in the gap, it is a conductor. If not, it is an insulator.

==================================================
SECTION: EXAMPLE
EXAMPLE_ID: EX_CI_03
TITLE: Power Line Insulators

DESCRIPTION:
Ceramic or glass discs on power poles prevent electricity from traveling into the ground.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: MC_CI_01
STATEMENT:
All water is a good conductor of electricity.

CORRECTION:
Pure water is a poor conductor. Impurities like salts allow electricity to flow.

SECTION: LEARNING_OBJECTIVES
- Define conductors and insulators.
- Explain why metals conduct electricity.
- Describe the role of electrons in electric current.
- Understand safety precautions with water and electricity.
- Explain the concept of resistance.

==================================================
SECTION: SUMMARY
Conductors allow electricity to flow because they contain free electrons, while insulators block electric current. Water and the human body can conduct electricity due to dissolved salts. Resistance affects how easily electricity flows and can produce heat. Understanding these principles helps ensure electrical safety and supports modern technology.
