1. Introduction to Our Environment
The environment is everything that surrounds us, including the land, water bodies, houses, plants, and animals. When we look around our school garden or home, we see many different things. Some of these things, like a child or a plant, grow over time. Others, like a pencil or a stone, stay the same size.


2. Defining Living Organisms
Growth is the increase in size, height, or amount of something.


Living Things (Organisms): Components of the environment that show growth are called living organisms.


Non-Living Things: Components that do not show growth are called non-living things.
Living organisms are generally categorized into two main groups: Plants and Animals.

3. Key Characteristics of Living Organisms
To identify if something is truly "living," scientists look for specific characteristics. While growth is the most obvious, there are several other vital processes:

Growth: All living things grow at some stage in their life. For example, a small seed becomes a giant tree, and a baby grows into an adult.

Nutrition: All organisms need energy to live. Animals get this by eating food, while plants produce their own food using sunlight.
+1

Respiration: Organisms break down food to produce energy. This usually involves taking in oxygen and releasing carbon dioxide.

Movement: Animals move from place to place to find food or safety. While plants don't walk, they show movement by turning their leaves toward the sun or opening their flowers.

Reproduction: To ensure their kind continues, living things produce offspring (babies or seeds).


Sensitivity: Living things respond to changes in their environment, such as a plant leaning toward light or a person shivering in the cold.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The Science of Classification (Taxonomy)
In Grade 6, we begin by separating the world into "Living" and "Non-living." However, scientists use a deeper system called Taxonomy to organize millions of species. This system was pioneered by Carolus Linnaeus. By grouping organisms based on shared characteristics, scientists can communicate clearly across different languages. For instance, while we say "Aliya" in Sinhala or "Elephant" in English, the scientific name Elephas maximus identifies the Asian Elephant globally.

Photosynthesis: The Miracle of Plant Life
Unlike animals, plants are "autotrophs," meaning they are self-feeders. They perform a unique process called photosynthesis. Using a green pigment called chlorophyll, plants trap energy from sunlight to convert water and carbon dioxide into glucose (food). This is why plants are the "producers" at the start of every food chain. Without this process, life on Earth would not have the oxygen we breathe or the primary food source we depend on.

The Cellular Basis of Life
While we see growth on the outside, the "magic" happens on the inside. Every living organism is made of tiny building blocks called cells. Some organisms, like bacteria, are made of just one cell (unicellular). Others, like humans and mango trees, are made of trillions of cells (multicellular). Growth occurs because these cells divide and create more cells. This is a fundamental difference between a living kitten and a non-living toy cat; the kitten's cells are constantly working, repairing, and multiplying.

Adaptation and Survival
Characteristics are not just about staying alive; they are about thriving in specific environments. This is called adaptation. In Sri Lanka, you might see plants like the Cactus in dry zones with thick stems to store water, while plants in the rainforest have "drip tips" on their leaves to shed excess rain quickly. Animals adapt too—the Sloth Bear has long claws to dig for termites, and the Blue Whale has a thick layer of blubber to stay warm in the ocean.

The Cycle of Nutrients
When we talk about nutrition and growth, we must also consider what happens at the end of life. Decomposers, such as fungi and bacteria, are the "recyclers" of the living world. They break down dead plants and animals, returning vital nutrients to the soil. This allows new plants to grow, continuing the cycle of the "Wonders of the Living World."

Sensitivity: The Five Senses and Beyond
Sensitivity (or irritability) is how organisms "sense" their world. Humans use five primary senses: sight, hearing, smell, taste, and touch. However, some organisms have "super-senses." For example, bats use echolocation (sound waves) to navigate in total darkness, and some snakes can "see" the heat coming off their prey. Understanding these diverse characteristics helps us appreciate the complexity of life in Sri Lankan ecosystems, from the central highlands to the coastal reefs.
