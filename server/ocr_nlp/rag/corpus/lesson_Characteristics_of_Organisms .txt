SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: Environment and Its Components
DEFINITION:
The environment is everything that surrounds us, including living organisms and non-living things such as land, water, and human-made objects.

KEY_POINTS:

Environment includes both living and non-living components.
Living components show growth and change over time.
Non-living components remain the same size.

EXAMPLES:
A school garden contains living organisms like plants and insects, along with non-living things like stones and buildings.

KEYWORDS:
environment, living things, non-living things, surroundings, components

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: Growth as a Defining Characteristic
DEFINITION:
Growth is the increase in size, height, or amount of something, and is a primary indicator used to distinguish living organisms from non-living things.

KEY_POINTS:

Growth means increasing in size or quantity.
Living organisms show growth at some stage in their life.
Non-living things do not grow on their own.

EXAMPLES:
A seed grows into a giant tree, and a baby grows into an adult, while a stone stays the same size forever.

KEYWORDS:
growth, increase, size, living indicator, development

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Living Organisms versus Non-Living Things
DEFINITION:
Living organisms are components of the environment that show growth and other life processes, while non-living things do not exhibit these characteristics.

KEY_POINTS:

Living organisms are categorized mainly as plants and animals.
Living things show growth and life processes.
Non-living things lack biological processes.

EXAMPLES:
A child and a plant are living organisms because they grow, while a pencil and a stone are non-living.

KEYWORDS:
living organisms, non-living things, plants, animals, classification

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C4
NAME: Six Characteristics of Living Organisms
DEFINITION:
Living organisms exhibit six key characteristics: growth, nutrition, respiration, movement, reproduction, and sensitivity.

KEY_POINTS:

Growth: All living things increase in size at some life stage.
Nutrition: Organisms need energy; animals eat food while plants make their own.
Respiration: Breaking down food to produce energy, usually involving oxygen intake and carbon dioxide release.
Movement: Animals move to find food or safety; plants move by turning toward sun or opening flowers.
Reproduction: Producing offspring to continue the species.
Sensitivity: Responding to environmental changes like light or temperature.

EXAMPLES:
A plant shows all six characteristics by growing taller, making food through photosynthesis, respiring, turning toward sunlight, producing seeds, and responding to touch.

KEYWORDS:
growth, nutrition, respiration, movement, reproduction, sensitivity, life processes

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C5
NAME: Taxonomy and Scientific Classification
DEFINITION:
Taxonomy is the scientific system for organizing and naming millions of species based on shared characteristics, pioneered by Carolus Linnaeus.

KEY_POINTS:

Taxonomy groups organisms by shared characteristics.
Scientific names allow clear communication across languages.
The system was developed by Carolus Linnaeus.

EXAMPLES:
Asian Elephant is called "Aliya" in Sinhala and "Elephant" in English, but the scientific name Elephas maximus is understood globally.

KEYWORDS:
taxonomy, classification, Carolus Linnaeus, scientific name, Elephas maximus, Aliya

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C6
NAME: Photosynthesis and Autotrophs
DEFINITION:
Photosynthesis is the process by which plants produce their own food using chlorophyll to convert sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose.

KEY_POINTS:

Plants are autotrophs, meaning self-feeders.
Chlorophyll is the green pigment that traps sunlight energy.
Photosynthesis produces glucose and oxygen.
Plants are producers at the start of every food chain.

EXAMPLES:
A tree uses chlorophyll in its leaves to capture sunlight and make glucose from water and carbon dioxide, releasing oxygen we breathe.

KEYWORDS:
photosynthesis, autotroph, chlorophyll, glucose, producers, food chain, oxygen

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C7
NAME: Cellular Basis of Life
DEFINITION:
All living organisms are made of tiny building blocks called cells, which divide to create more cells and enable growth.

KEY_POINTS:

Cells are the fundamental units of life.
Unicellular organisms like bacteria have just one cell.
Multicellular organisms like humans and trees have trillions of cells.
Growth happens through cell division and multiplication.

EXAMPLES:
A living kitten's cells constantly work, repair, and multiply, while a toy cat has no cells.

KEYWORDS:
cells, unicellular, multicellular, cell division, building blocks, bacteria

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C8
NAME: Adaptation to Environment
DEFINITION:
Adaptation is the process by which organisms develop characteristics that help them thrive in specific environments.

KEY_POINTS:

Adaptations help organisms survive in their habitat.
Physical features match environmental conditions.
Both plants and animals show adaptations.

EXAMPLES:
In Sri Lanka, cacti in dry zones have thick stems to store water, rainforest plants have drip tips to shed excess rain, Sloth Bears have long claws for digging termites, and Blue Whales have blubber for ocean warmth.

KEYWORDS:
adaptation, survival, habitat, dry zone, rainforest, Sloth Bear, Blue Whale, environment

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C9
NAME: Decomposers and Nutrient Cycling
DEFINITION:
Decomposers like fungi and bacteria break down dead organisms and return vital nutrients to the soil, continuing the cycle of life.

KEY_POINTS:

Decomposers are recyclers of the living world.
They break down dead plants and animals.
Nutrients return to soil for new plant growth.
This completes the cycle of life and death.

EXAMPLES:
Fungi break down a fallen tree, releasing nutrients into the soil that help new seedlings grow.

KEYWORDS:
decomposers, fungi, bacteria, nutrient cycle, recycling, soil nutrients

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C10
NAME: Sensitivity and Diverse Senses
DEFINITION:
Sensitivity is how organisms sense and respond to their environment, using various sensory mechanisms from basic five senses to specialized abilities.

KEY_POINTS:

Humans use five primary senses: sight, hearing, smell, taste, and touch.
Some organisms have super-senses beyond human abilities.
Sensitivity helps organisms respond to environmental changes.

EXAMPLES:
Bats use echolocation with sound waves to navigate in darkness, and some snakes detect heat from prey, helping them survive in Sri Lankan ecosystems from highlands to coastal reefs.

KEYWORDS:
sensitivity, senses, echolocation, heat detection, environmental response, Sri Lankan ecosystems

SECTION: SUMMARY
Living organisms are distinguished from non-living things through six key characteristics: growth, nutrition, respiration, movement, reproduction, and sensitivity. The environment contains both living components that grow and change, and non-living components that remain static. Scientific classification through taxonomy, pioneered by Carolus Linnaeus, allows organisms to be named systematically across languages, such as using Elephas maximus for the Asian Elephant known as "Aliya" in Sinhala. Plants are autotrophs that produce their own food through photosynthesis using chlorophyll to convert sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose and oxygen. All living organisms are built from cells—some unicellular like bacteria, others multicellular like humans and trees—and growth occurs through cell division. Organisms develop adaptations to thrive in specific environments, such as cacti storing water in Sri Lankan dry zones, rainforest plants with drip tips, Sloth Bears with claws for digging, and Blue Whales with insulating blubber. Decomposers like fungi and bacteria recycle nutrients by breaking down dead organisms, returning vital elements to soil for new plant growth. Sensitivity ranges from human five senses to specialized abilities like bat echolocation and snake heat detection, supporting survival across diverse Sri Lankan ecosystems from central highlands to coastal reefs.
