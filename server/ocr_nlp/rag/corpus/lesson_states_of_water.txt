Water is the most important natural resource provided to us by nature. It is essential for the survival of all living things. While we commonly think of water as a liquid found in rivers, streams, and wells, it actually exists in more than one form.+13.1 States of WaterIn the natural environment, water exists in three physical states: solid, liquid, and gas.1. Water in Solid StateExamples: Ice, snow, and glaciers.Locations: Glaziers (large masses of ice) are found in the Northern and Southern poles of the world. In colder countries, snow is seen during the winter season.2. Water in Liquid StateExamples: The water in lakes, rivers, streams, tanks (reservoirs), ponds, wells, and the sea.Daily Life: In our language, the word "water" usually refers specifically to this liquid state.3. Water in Gaseous StateExamples: Steam and water vapour.Location: Water exists in the atmosphere around us as invisible water vapour.+1Demonstration: If you put ice cubes into a glass, you will see water droplets form on the outside. This is because the water vapour in the air cools down (condenses) when it touches the cold glass.Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)The Science of Changing States: The Energy LinkWhat causes water to change from a solid to a liquid or from a liquid to a gas? The answer is heat energy. When we add heat to ice (solid), the particles inside begin to move faster until they break free from their rigid structure and become liquid water. This is called melting. If we continue to add heat, the water boils and turns into water vapour (gas). This is called evaporation. Conversely, removing heat (cooling) causes water vapour to turn back into liquid (condensation) and liquid to turn into solid ice (freezing). This cycle of energy is what drives the global climate.+3Water: The Universal SolventOne of the most unique properties of water is its ability to dissolve many different substances. This is why it is called a "universal solvent." Because of this, water is never purely $H_2O$ in nature. As it flows through rivers or sits in the ocean, it dissolves minerals, salts, and gases. This is why seawater tastes salty—it contains dissolved sodium chloride. This property is vital for life; for example, the water in our blood dissolves nutrients and carries them to every cell in our body.+3The "Anomalous" Expansion of WaterMost substances shrink when they get cold and turn into solids. Water is a rare exception. When water freezes into ice, it actually expands and becomes less dense. This is why ice cubes float in a glass of water rather than sinking. In nature, this is life-saving: in frozen lakes, the ice forms on the top surface, acting as an insulating blanket that keeps the liquid water underneath warm enough for fish and plants to survive during winter.Water and the Sri Lankan EnvironmentIn Sri Lanka, water is not just a resource; it is the foundation of our history and agriculture. Our ancestors built complex "Tank Systems" (Wewa) to manage liquid water for rice cultivation. Furthermore, the gaseous state of water (humidity) is very high in our country because we are an island surrounded by the Indian Ocean. This high humidity is what makes the air feel "sticky" on a hot day in Colombo or Kandy.The Water Cycle: Nature’s Recycling PlantThe three states of water work together in a continuous loop called the Water Cycle.Evaporation: The sun heats the liquid water in our oceans and rivers, turning it into water vapour (gas).Condensation: This vapour rises into the atmosphere, cools down, and forms clouds (liquid droplets).+1Precipitation: When the droplets get too heavy, they fall back to Earth as rain (liquid) or snow/hail (solid).
