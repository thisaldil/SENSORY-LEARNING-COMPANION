Types of Water based on Availability
Water is found in different places in our environment. Based on where it is located and how we access it, we can divide it into three main types:

1. Surface Water
This is the water that is found on the surface of the earth. It is the most visible form of water.

Examples: Streams, rivers, ponds, lakes, and the sea.

In Sri Lanka: We have many "Tanks" (Wewa) built by ancient kings, which are important sources of surface water for agriculture.

2. Ground Water
Some of the rain that falls on the ground seeps through the soil and collects between underground rocks. This is called ground water.

Access: We get this water by digging wells or using tube wells.

Characteristics: Ground water is usually filtered naturally as it passes through layers of sand and soil, but it can still contain dissolved minerals.

3. Atmospheric Water
Water is also present in the air around us.

Forms: It exists as water vapour (gas), and as tiny water droplets in clouds and mist.

Visibility: While water vapour is invisible, we can see it when it turns into clouds or as dew on grass in the morning.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The Science of Aquifers: Nature’s Underground Storage
Ground water doesn't just sit in big empty caves; it mostly fills the tiny pores and cracks in rocks and soil. A layer of rock that holds a significant amount of ground water is called an aquifer. In the northern parts of Sri Lanka, like Jaffna, people rely almost entirely on limestone aquifers because there are very few rivers. Protecting this ground water is vital because once it becomes polluted by chemicals or salt water, it is very difficult and expensive to clean.

Surface Water: Freshwater vs. Marine Water
Not all surface water is the same. Scientists divide it into:

Freshwater: This has very low salt content. Only about 3% of the world's water is fresh, and most of that is trapped in ice caps. Our rivers and tanks are our primary source of freshwater.

Marine Water: This is the salty water in our oceans and seas. While we cannot drink it or use it for most plants, it is home to millions of sea creatures and helps regulate the Earth's temperature.

Brackish Water: This is a mixture of fresh and salt water, usually found in lagoons where rivers meet the sea. Sri Lanka has many beautiful lagoons, like the Negombo Lagoon, which are rich in biodiversity.

The Movement of Atmospheric Water
Atmospheric water is the "engine" of our weather. When the sun heats the ocean, water molecules speed up and escape into the air as vapour. This process is called evaporation. As this vapour rises into the cooler parts of the atmosphere, it slows down and sticks to tiny dust particles, forming clouds. This is called condensation. In Sri Lanka, our "monsoon" seasons are driven by massive amounts of atmospheric water being carried by winds across the ocean and falling as rain over the island.

The Water Table and its Importance
If you dig a hole in the ground, you will eventually reach a point where the soil is completely soaked with water. The top level of this underground water is called the Water Table. During the rainy season in Sri Lanka, the water table rises, making it easier to get water from wells. In the dry season, the water table drops. If we pump too much water out of the ground (over-extraction), the water table can drop so low that wells go dry and plants cannot reach moisture with their roots.

Water Quality and Human Impact
As water moves through these different "availabilities," its quality can change. Rainwater (atmospheric) is generally pure but can pick up pollutants from factory smoke. Surface water is easily contaminated by plastic waste and factory chemicals. Even ground water can be affected by the overuse of fertilizers in farming. In many parts of Sri Lanka’s North Central Province, scientists are studying how ground water quality affects human health, such as Kidney disease (CKDu), highlighting why protecting our water sources is a national priority.
