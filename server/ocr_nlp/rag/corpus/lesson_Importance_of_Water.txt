SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: Water as the Foundation of Life
DEFINITION:
Water is the single most important substance for all life on Earth, absolutely essential for survival as every cell in every living organism is composed mostly of water, and human bodies can only survive 3-5 days without water compared to weeks without food or hours without oxygen.

KEY_POINTS:

Water is more essential than food or oxygen for survival.
Your body can survive only 3-5 days without water.
Every cell in every living thing is composed mostly of water.
Water acts like blood for Earth, carrying and supporting everything life needs.
Without water the planet would become a dead rock.
Water is the universal medium enabling all biological processes.

EXAMPLES:
Unlike food where humans can survive weeks without eating or oxygen where survival is possible for several hours without breathing, water is so critical that your body can only function for 3-5 days without it because every life process depends on water.

KEYWORDS:
water foundation, life essential, cellular water, survival requirement, universal medium, biological necessity, critical substance

SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: Water in Living Organisms - Plants
DEFINITION:
Plants are approximately 90-96% water, with water serving four critical functions: providing the medium for photosynthesis where water molecules combine with carbon dioxide to capture sunlight's energy creating glucose, dissolving and transporting minerals from soil through roots to leaves, maintaining structural rigidity and shape in plant cells, and regulating temperature through transpiration where water evaporates from leaves cooling the plant.

KEY_POINTS:

Plants are 90-96% water by mass.
Water provides the medium for photosynthesis combining with CO₂ and capturing sunlight energy.
Water dissolves soil minerals and transports them from roots to leaves through xylem vessels.
Water fills plant cells creating structural support and rigidity (wilted plants droop when losing water).
Transpiration is water evaporating from leaves cooling the plant.
Without water, plants instantly begin to die.

EXAMPLES:
Tomato plants are 95% water, lettuce is 96% water, and even "dry" seeds require water to germinate and grow, with seeds remaining dormant forever without water availability.

KEYWORDS:
plant water percentage, photosynthesis medium, mineral transport, xylem, structural support, transpiration, wilting, plant hydration

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Water in Living Organisms - Animals
DEFINITION:
Animals are 50-70% water depending on species, with water serving six critical functions: providing the medium for digestion where stomach acid mixed with water breaks down food, transporting oxygen and nutrients throughout the body via blood which is 92% water, filtering waste products through kidneys to create urine, cooling the body through sweating controlled evaporation, lubricating joints with water-based fluid, and protecting the brain and spinal cord with cerebrospinal fluid.

KEY_POINTS:

Animals are 50-70% water depending on species.
Brain is 73% water.
Blood is 92% water carrying oxygen and nutrients everywhere.
Lungs are 83% water.
Stomach and intestines are 75% water.
Water provides digestive medium breaking down food with stomach acid.
Kidneys use water to filter waste and create urine.
Sweating (controlled water evaporation) cools the body.
Water-based fluid lubricates joints allowing smooth movement.
Cerebrospinal fluid (water-based) cushions and protects brain and spinal cord.
Without water body cannot cool itself leading to heat stroke.
Dehydration happens faster in hot dry climates like Sri Lanka's dry zone.

EXAMPLES:
Your brain is 73% water and becomes impaired when dehydrated preventing clear thinking, and your blood is 92% water becoming too thick during dehydration forcing your heart to work harder, demonstrating how water is essential for basic body functioning.

KEYWORDS:
animal water percentage, brain water, blood water, digestion, nutrient transport, waste removal, kidney function, urine, sweating, temperature regulation, lubrication, cerebrospinal fluid, protection, dehydration, heat stroke

NAME: Water in Agriculture
DEFINITION:
Agriculture is the largest global user of freshwater consuming 70% of all freshwater, with rice paddy cultivation in Sri Lanka requiring 1000-2000mm water per season from monsoon rains, irrigation tanks (wewa), underground water, and rivers, with over 70% of Sri Lankans in rural areas depending on agriculture for survival and employment.

KEY_POINTS:

Agriculture uses 70% of global freshwater.
Rice paddies need 1000-2000 millimeters water per season.
Water sources for paddy cultivation include monsoon rain, irrigation tanks (wewa), underground water, and rivers.
Water timing is critical - water must arrive at correct season or crops fail.
Flooding fields maintains moisture during growth and harvesting periods.
Other agricultural uses include vegetable farming, fruit orchards, livestock watering, and aquaculture/fish farming.
Over 70% of Sri Lankans in rural areas depend on agriculture employment.
Agriculture provides 10% of Sri Lanka's national GDP.
If water fails rural economy collapses causing national food crisis.
Ancient Sri Lankan kings built wewa tank systems to store monsoon water enabling dry season farming.

EXAMPLES:
Rice farmer in Anuradhapura planning rice crop requires April-May monsoon flooding fields (5-10 cm water), May-August regular flooding maintaining water level as rice grows (total 1500-2000 liters per cubic meter), and August water drainage for harvesting, with failure at any stage resulting in no harvest, stunted rice, or poor grain quality affecting national food security.

KEYWORDS:
agriculture water usage, rice cultivation, paddy fields, monsoon, irrigation, wewa tanks, water timing, crop failure, dry season, rural economy, Sri Lanka farming, food security

SECTION: EXAMPLE
EXAMPLE_ID: E1
TITLE: Water in Your Body - Dehydration Effects

DESCRIPTION:
Your body weight is approximately 50 kg (typical 11-year-old) and 65% is water equaling 32.5 kg of water distributed throughout: blood 3 liters (92% water), brain 1.5 kg (73% water), lungs 1 kg (83% water), stomach and intestines 2 kg (75% water). When you lose water percentages have serious effects: losing 1% (500 ml) causes thirst, losing 5% (2.5 liters) causes weakness and dizziness, losing 10% (5 liters) causes heat stroke and possible death, losing 20% (10 liters) causes death. In Sri Lanka's hot climate playing sports like running for one hour causes you to lose 1-2 liters of sweat, and if you don't replace it within 30 minutes you experience dehydration symptoms including impaired thinking, muscle cramps, and heart strain.

SECTION: EXAMPLE
EXAMPLE_ID: E2
TITLE: Rice Farmer's Water Dependency in Anuradhapura

DESCRIPTION:
A rice farmer in Anuradhapura planning a rice crop must manage critical water timing across four stages: April-May monsoon begins and fields must be flooded with 5-10 cm of water for seeds to germinate, May-August consistent flooding maintains water level as rice plants grow requiring total of 1500-2000 liters per cubic meter, August monsoon ends and water supply stops allowing fields to dry for harvesting. If water fails at initial flooding stage no seeds germinate producing no crop, if water fails during growing season rice becomes stunted producing low yield, if harvest timing is wrong grain quality is damaged destroying income. Failure at any stage affects not just one farmer but national food security since rice is Sri Lanka's staple crop and 70% of rural population depends on agricultural employment.

SECTION: EXAMPLE
EXAMPLE_ID: E3
TITLE: Water Consumption in Cotton T-Shirt Production

DESCRIPTION:
Manufacturing a single cotton T-shirt requires 2700 liters of water to grow the cotton fiber in cotton fields plus 300 liters for processing the fiber into fabric and dyeing it the desired color, totaling 3000 liters of water per shirt. A small Sri Lankan textile factory with 100 workers producing 500 shirts daily requires 500 × 3000 = 1,500,000 liters of water per day, equal to the drinking water supply for 15,000 people for one full day. If the factory operates 300 days per year the annual water consumption is 450,000,000 liters, demonstrating the massive industrial water demand competing directly with domestic and agricultural water needs.

SECTION: EXAMPLE
EXAMPLE_ID: E4
TITLE: Ancient Sri Lankan Water Systems - King Parakramabahu's Wewa Tanks

DESCRIPTION:
In the 12th century King Parakramabahu the Great built massive storage tanks called "wewa" connected through channels and canals to create an integrated water management system that stored monsoon rains and released water during dry seasons for paddy cultivation. These ancient tanks still function today over 850 years later, with some still actively supplying water to farms and communities demonstrating the ingenuity of traditional Sri Lankan hydraulic engineering. The wewa system transformed dry zones into agricultural regions enabling large populations to survive in areas that would otherwise be too dry for farming, with historical populations reaching over 2 million people supported by these water systems. Modern Sri Lanka still relies on many ancient wewa tanks alongside newer dams like Victoria, Randenigala, and Kotmale, showing that traditional water knowledge remains as important today as 800 years ago.

SECTION: EXAMPLE
EXAMPLE_ID: E5
TITLE: Sri Lanka's Competing Water Demands During Dry Season

DESCRIPTION:
Sri Lanka's daily water needs create a complex competition between different users during dry season: domestic water needs 2 billion liters daily (drinking, bathing, sanitation for millions), agricultural needs 8 billion liters daily (paddy fields, plantations, irrigation), industrial needs 3 billion liters daily (factories, tea processing, textile mills, coconut oil extraction), and environmental needs 2 billion liters daily (rivers, wetlands, fisheries, ecosystems). Total daily demand equals 15 billion liters, but available supply during dry season is only 12 billion liters, creating a deficit of 3 billion liters daily. Solutions include reducing agricultural water waste through drip irrigation instead of flood irrigation, recycling industrial water instead of discarding it, restricting urban sprawl in water-scarce areas, protecting existing wewa tanks and aquifers from pollution, and promoting water conservation practices that reduce individual consumption.

SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M1
STATEMENT:
Because Earth is mostly covered in water, we have unlimited water available for drinking and agriculture.

CORRECTION:
While Earth is covered in water, 97% is salt water that cannot be used for drinking or agriculture. Of the 3% that is fresh water, most is frozen in glaciers or deep underground. Only 0.3% of Earth's fresh water is accessible and available for human use, and when you account for water already used by ecosystems, only 0.01% is available for all human needs globally.
