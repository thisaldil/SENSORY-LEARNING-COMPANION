5.1 How can we see?
To see the world around us, two main factors must be fulfilled. Without both working together, vision is impossible.

1. A Source of Light
Light is essential for vision. During the day, we can see things clearly because of the sun. At night, we need to switch on lights like bulbs, lamps, or candles to see objects. If there is no light (for example, in a completely dark room), we cannot see anything even if our eyes are open.
+2

2. The Eye
Even if there is plenty of light, we cannot see if our eyes are closed. The eye is the organ that receives light and allows us to perceive our surroundings.
+2


Conclusion: Vision is the result of light from a source entering our eyes.

The Science of Reflection: How Objects Become Visible
You might wonder how we see things like a chair or a book, which do not produce their own light. This happens through a process called reflection. When light from a source (like a bulb or the sun) hits an object, the light bounces off the surface of that object in different directions. When this bounced light enters our eyes, our brain processes the information, and we "see" the object. Smooth surfaces like mirrors reflect light very well, while rough surfaces like a piece of wood scatter light in many directions.

Luminous vs. Non-Luminous Objects
Scientists categorize objects based on whether they can produce their own light:

Luminous Objects: These are "light makers." They produce and emit their own light.
+1


Natural examples: The Sun, stars, fireflies, and even some special mushrooms.
+1


Artificial examples: A lit candle, an electric bulb, and a glowing flashlight.
+1

Non-Luminous Objects: These objects cannot produce their own light. We only see them because they reflect light from a luminous source.


Examples: Tables, chairs, the moon, and planets.
+2


Note: The moon looks bright at night, but it is actually non-luminous; it is just reflecting the sun's light back to Earth.

The Human Eye: A Natural Camera
The eye works much like a high-tech camera. Light enters through a clear front layer called the cornea and passes through a hole called the pupil. The iris (the colored part of your eye) acts like a shutter, getting smaller in bright light to protect the eye and larger in the dark to let more light in. A lens inside the eye focuses the light onto the retina at the back, which sends signals to the brain through the optic nerve. This process is nearly instant, allowing us to see movement and colors in real-time.

Vision and Light in Different Environments
In Sri Lanka, we experience very bright sunlight during the day. Our eyes adapt by making the pupils small. However, when we enter a dark room or a movie theater, it takes a few moments for our eyes to "adjust." This is because our pupils need time to widen to catch as much of the limited light as possible. This is a survival characteristic that helps humans and animals navigate in low-light conditions.

Optical Illusions: When the Brain is Tricked
Sometimes, our eyes and brain can be tricked. Because our brain is used to light traveling in straight lines, it can be confused when light bends (refracts). Have you ever noticed how a straw looks broken when you put it in a glass of water? This is an optical illusion. The light bends as it moves from the water to the air, making the straw appear to be in a different place than it actually is.

Protecting Your Vision
Since the eye is a delicate organ, we must protect it to maintain good vision:

Never look directly at the sun: The intense light can permanently damage your retina.

Read in good light: Reading in the dark strains the eye muscles.

Keep a safe distance from screens: Whether it's a TV, computer, or phone, staring too closely for too long can cause eye fatigue.
