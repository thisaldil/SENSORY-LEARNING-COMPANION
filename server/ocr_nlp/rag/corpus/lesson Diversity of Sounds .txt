Diversity of Sounds
The world around us is filled with a vast variety of sounds. We do not hear the same sound everywhere; instead, sounds differ based on how they are produced and the objects that create them. We can classify the diversity of sounds into several categories.

1. Natural Sounds vs. Artificial Sounds
Natural Sounds: These are produced by nature without human interference. Examples include the roar of a waterfall, the rustling of leaves in the wind, the thunder during a storm, and the songs of birds like the Kohila.

Artificial Sounds: These are man-made sounds created by machines or human activities. Examples include the sound of a vehicle engine, a radio playing music, a hammer hitting a nail, or a school bell ringing.

2. Pleasant vs. Unpleasant Sounds
Pleasant Sounds: Sounds that are soothing and give us a sense of comfort. Examples include soft music, the sound of a flute, or the gentle rippling of a stream.

Unpleasant Sounds (Noise): Sounds that are harsh, loud, or disturbing to our ears. Examples include the screeching of tires, loud construction work, or the blaring of a horn in traffic.

3. High-Pitched vs. Low-Pitched Sounds
Sounds also differ in their "tone." A bird’s chirp is a high-pitched sound, while the growl of a lion or the beat of a large "Daula" drum is a low-pitched sound.

The Physics of Sound Diversity: Amplitude and Frequency
To understand why sounds are diverse, we must look at the vibrations themselves. There are two main properties that change a sound:

Amplitude (Loudness): This depends on the strength of the vibration. If you hit a drum hard, the membrane moves a great distance, creating a loud sound with high amplitude. If you tap it gently, the amplitude is low, and the sound is soft.

Frequency (Pitch): This is how fast the object vibrates. High-frequency vibrations create high-pitched sounds (like a whistle), while low-frequency vibrations create low-pitched sounds (like a bass guitar).

The Concept of "Timbre" or Quality
Have you ever wondered why a piano and a violin sound different even if they play the exact same note at the same loudness? This is because of a property called Timbre (Sound Quality). Every object has a unique way of vibrating based on its material and shape. A wooden flute produces "smoother" waves, while a metal trumpet produces "sharper" waves. This diversity allows our brains to distinguish between a mother’s voice and a teacher’s voice without even seeing them.

Sound Diversity in Sri Lankan Musical Instruments
Sri Lanka has a rich heritage of musical instruments that demonstrate the diversity of sound.

Percussion (Awanaddha Mangalya): Instruments like the Geta Beraya and Yak Beraya use stretched animal skins. The tension of the skin changes the pitch.

Wind (Sushira): The Horanewa (a traditional oboe-like instrument) uses a vibrating reed and a column of air to create high-pitched, piercing sounds used in ceremonies.

String (Taththa): Though rarer in traditional folk, instruments like the Vinawa use vibrating strings.
The different materials used—wood, clay, skin, and metal—all contribute to the unique "soundscape" of Sri Lankan music.

Animal Communication and Sound Diversity
Animals use the diversity of sound to survive. In the Sinharaja forest, different species of birds sing at different pitches so they don't "jam" each other's signals. It is like having different radio stations. Some insects, like crickets, produce sound by rubbing their legs together (stridulation), creating a rhythmic "chirp" that is very different from the vocal sounds of mammals. This variety ensures that a mate can find them even in a noisy forest.

The Danger of Noise Pollution
When sound diversity is lost to constant, loud, artificial noise, we call it Noise Pollution. In busy cities like Colombo, the constant "unpleasant" sounds of traffic and machinery can lead to stress, high blood pressure, and hearing loss in humans. It also interferes with animals; for example, songbirds in cities have been found to sing louder or at higher pitches just to be heard over the noise of buses and cars.

Ultrasound and Infrasound: The Hidden Diversity
There is a whole world of sound that we cannot even hear. Ultrasound consists of very high-frequency sounds used by bats to "see" in the dark. Infrasound consists of very low-frequency sounds that can travel through mountains and oceans. Elephants use these low rumbles to talk to friends who are many kilometers away. Even though these are "silent" to us, they are a vital part of the diversity of sounds on our planet.
