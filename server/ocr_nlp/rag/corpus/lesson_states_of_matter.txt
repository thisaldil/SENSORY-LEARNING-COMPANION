The Kinetic Nature of Matter
The state of any given substance is primarily determined by the balance between the kinetic energy of its particles and the intermolecular forces pulling them together. In solids, low kinetic energy allows strong bonds to hold atoms in a rigid, defined structure with minimal vibration. As heat is added, particles gain enough energy to overcome some of these attractions, transitioning into a liquid state where they can flow and take the shape of their container while remaining condensed. If energy continues to increase, the particles break free entirely, entering the gaseous state where they move rapidly and independently through space.

Phase Transitions and Energy Exchange
Matter transitions between states through the addition or removal of thermal energy, processes known as phase changes. When a substance moves from a lower energy state to a higher one—such as melting (solid to liquid) or evaporation (liquid to gas)—it absorbs energy from its surroundings. Conversely, freezing and condensation release energy. Under specific conditions of temperature and pressure, some substances can even bypass the liquid phase entirely through sublimation or deposition. These transitions are fundamental to natural cycles, such as the movement of water through Earth's atmosphere and oceans.
