SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_ELECTRICITY_IMPORTANCE
NAME: Electricity as Essential Energy
DEFINITION:
Electricity is a form of energy that provides invisible power for modern life, helping people live comfortably, work efficiently, and communicate.

KEY_POINTS:

- Electricity is used in homes, schools, hospitals, and industries
- It supports comfort, productivity, and daily communication
- Many modern devices depend on electricity to function

EXAMPLES:
Without electricity, lights, phone charging, refrigeration, and many school or office activities stop.

KEYWORDS:
electricity, energy, daily life, modern world, power, comfort, communication

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_DAILY_APPLICATIONS
NAME: Applications of Electricity in Daily Activities
DEFINITION:
Electricity is converted into other forms of energy such as light, heat, motion, and signals to perform useful tasks in day-to-day life.

KEY_POINTS:

- Lighting devices include bulbs, tube lights, and streetlights
- Heating devices include electric kettles, irons, and rice cookers
- Cooling devices include fans and air conditioners, important in warm Sri Lankan weather
- Movement devices include washing machines, blenders, and electric fans
- Communication devices include mobile phone chargers, televisions, and computers

EXAMPLES:
An electric rice cooker converts electrical energy to heat, while a washing machine converts electrical energy to motion.

KEYWORDS:
lighting, heating, cooling, motion, communication, household appliances, energy conversion

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_ELECTRICITY_SOURCES
NAME: Sources of Electricity in Sri Lanka
DEFINITION:
Electricity is generated at power stations and transmitted through wires to homes and buildings. Sri Lanka uses multiple energy sources to produce electricity.

KEY_POINTS:

- Hydropower uses falling water to drive turbines and generate electricity
- Thermal power uses heat from burning coal or oil
- Wind power uses moving air to turn turbines
- Solar power uses sunlight to produce electrical energy
- Different sources improve reliability of national electricity supply

EXAMPLES:
Sri Lanka gets major electricity from hydropower stations and also uses wind in Mannar and solar projects in Hambantota.

KEYWORDS:
power stations, hydropower, thermal power, wind energy, solar energy, electricity generation, Sri Lanka

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_CURRENT_AND_CIRCUIT
NAME: Electric Current and Circuits
DEFINITION:
Electric current is the flow of electrons through a conductor. For current to flow and make a device work, there must be a complete path called a circuit.

KEY_POINTS:

- Electrons are tiny particles that move in conductors such as copper wire
- A complete circuit is required for electrical devices to operate
- If a wire is broken, current cannot flow
- If a switch is OFF, the circuit is open and the appliance stops working

EXAMPLES:
A torch lights up only when the battery, bulb, and switch form a complete circuit.

KEYWORDS:
electric current, electrons, conductor, circuit, open circuit, closed circuit, switch

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_CONDUCTORS_INSULATORS
NAME: Conductors and Insulators
DEFINITION:
Materials are grouped by how easily they allow electric current to pass. Conductors allow current flow, while insulators resist or block it.

KEY_POINTS:

- Copper, silver, and aluminum are good conductors
- Rubber, plastic, and glass are insulators
- Copper is widely used for internal wire cores
- Plastic coverings on wires help protect people from electric shock

EXAMPLES:
A household cable has a copper core to carry current and a plastic outer layer for safety.

KEYWORDS:
conductors, insulators, copper, aluminum, rubber, plastic coating, electric safety

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_MAINS_VS_BATTERY
NAME: Mains Electricity and Battery Power
DEFINITION:
Homes use two common electricity sources: mains electricity from wall sockets and stored chemical electricity from cells or batteries.

KEY_POINTS:

- Mains electricity provides high power through wall sockets
- Mains electricity can be dangerous if used carelessly
- Cells and batteries provide lower-voltage portable power
- Batteries are used in torches, clocks, and remote controls
- Batteries need recharging or replacement after stored energy is used

EXAMPLES:
A television usually uses mains electricity, while a remote control uses batteries.

KEYWORDS:
mains electricity, wall socket, batteries, cells, portable power, voltage, electrical supply

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_ELECTRICITY_COST_EFFICIENCY
NAME: Electricity Cost and Efficient Use
DEFINITION:
Electricity use is measured and billed in units of energy. Efficient use reduces cost and saves resources.

KEY_POINTS:

- Leaving lights or appliances on unnecessarily wastes energy
- Electricity bills depend on total units consumed
- Energy-efficient appliances use less electricity for the same task
- LED bulbs generally use less energy than old filament bulbs

EXAMPLES:
Replacing filament bulbs with LED bulbs can reduce household electricity bills.

KEYWORDS:
electricity bill, units, energy efficiency, LED bulbs, energy saving, responsible usage

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_ELECTRICITY_ENVIRONMENT
NAME: Electricity and the Environment
DEFINITION:
How electricity is produced affects the environment. Fossil-fuel generation can increase pollution and global warming, while renewable sources reduce these impacts.

KEY_POINTS:

- Burning coal and oil for power releases pollutants
- Fossil-fuel electricity contributes to global warming
- Renewable sources include hydropower, wind, and solar
- Renewable energy supports cleaner long-term electricity supply
- Sri Lanka is increasing renewable projects for sustainability

EXAMPLES:
Wind turbines in Mannar and solar farms in Hambantota help produce cleaner electricity.

KEYWORDS:
environment, fossil fuels, pollution, global warming, renewable energy, sustainability, Mannar, Hambantota

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_ELECTRICAL_SAFETY
NAME: Electrical Safety Principles
DEFINITION:
Electricity can be dangerous, so safety rules are essential to prevent electric shocks, fires, and equipment damage.

KEY_POINTS:

- Never touch switches or sockets with wet hands
- Do not overload one socket with too many plugs
- Keep metal objects away from socket holes
- Do not use damaged or frayed wires
- Water can increase shock risk because it helps current travel

EXAMPLES:
A frayed charging cable should be replaced immediately to reduce shock and fire risk.

KEYWORDS:
electrical safety, electric shock, wet hands, overloaded sockets, frayed wires, home safety

SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: CC_HISTORY_SRI_LANKA_ELECTRICITY
NAME: Early Development of Electricity in Sri Lanka
DEFINITION:
Sri Lanka’s electricity development began in the late 1800s and expanded through hydropower planning led by key national engineers.

KEY_POINTS:

- The first electric light in Colombo was lit in 1895
- D.J. Wimalasurendra is known as the Father of Hydropower in Sri Lanka
- Hydropower development used rivers and waterfalls to generate electricity
- Major projects such as Laxapana and Victoria became important power sources

EXAMPLES:
Hydropower plants influenced by Wimalasurendra’s vision still support Sri Lanka’s electricity system.

KEYWORDS:
Sri Lanka electricity history, Colombo 1895, D.J. Wimalasurendra, hydropower, Laxapana, Victoria

SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: MISC_ELECTRICITY_VISIBLE
STATEMENT:
Electricity is visible energy that we can always see directly.

CORRECTION:
Electricity is usually invisible. We observe its effects, such as a glowing bulb, a spinning fan, or a heated iron.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: MISC_CURRENT_WITHOUT_CIRCUIT
STATEMENT:
A device can work even if the circuit is incomplete.

CORRECTION:
Electrical devices require a complete path for electron flow. A broken wire or OFF switch opens the circuit and stops current.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: MISC_ALL_MATERIALS_CONDUCT
STATEMENT:
All materials allow electricity to pass equally.

CORRECTION:
Only conductors allow easy current flow. Insulators such as rubber and plastic resist current and are used for safety.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: MISC_BATTERY_ALWAYS_SAFE
STATEMENT:
Battery-powered devices are always safe and need no precautions.

CORRECTION:
Batteries are generally lower voltage than mains supply, but misuse can still cause heating, leakage, or short circuits. Safe handling and correct battery use are still necessary.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: MISC_RENEWABLE_NO_IMPORTANCE
STATEMENT:
It does not matter whether electricity comes from fossil fuels or renewable energy.

CORRECTION:
Generation source strongly affects pollution and climate impact. Increasing renewable electricity helps reduce air pollution and supports long-term sustainability.

SECTION: SUMMARY
Electricity is an essential form of energy that powers lighting, heating, cooling, movement, and communication in daily life. It is generated at power stations and delivered through networks, with Sri Lanka using hydropower, thermal power, wind, and solar sources. Electric current is the flow of electrons through a conductor, and devices work only when a complete circuit exists. Conductors such as copper carry current, while insulators such as plastic improve safety by limiting accidental contact with current. Homes use both mains electricity and battery power for different needs. Because electricity use is billed in energy units, efficient appliances and careful habits help save money. Electricity generation also affects the environment, so expanding renewables such as Mannar wind and Hambantota solar supports cleaner development. Since electricity can cause shock and fire hazards, strict safety practices—dry hands, safe sockets, and undamaged wires—are essential.
