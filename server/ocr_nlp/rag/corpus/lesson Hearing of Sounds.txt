Hearing of Sounds
While many things in our environment produce vibrations, we can only perceive them as "sound" because of our sense of hearing. The primary organ responsible for this is the ear. The ear is a delicate and complex organ that acts like a receiver for vibrations traveling through the air. It captures these vibrations and turns them into signals that our brain can understand.

The process of hearing involves several steps. First, the outer part of the ear, known as the pinna, acts like a funnel to collect sound waves from the environment. These waves travel through the ear canal and hit a thin membrane called the eardrum, causing it to vibrate. These vibrations then move three tiny bones in the middle ear which amplify the sound and pass it to the inner ear. In the inner ear, a snail-shaped part called the cochlea turns these vibrations into electrical signals. Finally, the auditory nerve carries these signals to the brain, which identifies the sound as music, a voice, or a warning signal.

The Human Auditory Range
Not all vibrations are audible to humans. Our ears are "tuned" to a specific range of frequencies. A healthy young person can usually hear sounds ranging from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Sounds below 20 Hz are called infrasound and are too low for us to hear, while sounds above 20,000 Hz are called ultrasound and are too high for our ears to detect. As people get older, their ability to hear high-frequency sounds usually decreases. This is a natural part of aging, but it can be accelerated by constant exposure to loud noises.

Hearing in the Animal Kingdom
Many animals have a much better sense of hearing than humans because they rely on it for survival. For example, bats and dolphins use ultrasound to navigate and find food. They emit high-pitched clicks that bounce off objects, and by hearing the "echo," they know exactly where an object is located. This process is called echolocation. On the other end of the scale, elephants can hear infrasound—very low frequencies that travel through the ground over long distances. This allows them to communicate with other herds kilometers away, even when they are out of sight. Dogs also have superior hearing, catching frequencies much higher than a human ear can detect, which is why they react to "silent" whistles.

Sound Localization: Why We Have Two Ears
Having an ear on each side of our head is not just for symmetry; it helps with sound localization, which is the ability to tell where a sound is coming from. If a friend calls your name from the right side, the sound reaches your right ear a tiny fraction of a second before it reaches your left ear. It is also slightly louder in the right ear. Your brain calculates these tiny differences in time and volume to tell you exactly where to look. This is a vital safety feature that helps us identify the direction of approaching vehicles or animals in the wild.

Protecting Your Hearing
Because the eardrum and the tiny bones in the ear are so delicate, they can be easily damaged. Constant exposure to loud music through headphones or living near noisy construction sites can wear out the tiny hair cells in the cochlea. Unlike some other cells in our body, once these hair cells are damaged, they do not grow back, leading to permanent hearing loss. It is also very important never to put sharp objects, pencils, or even cotton buds deep into your ear. You could accidentally tear your eardrum, which would stop it from vibrating and could cause deafness. In the Sri Lankan climate, swimming in polluted water can also lead to ear infections, so it is important to dry your ears and seek medical help if you feel pain.

The Speed of Sound vs. Light
Sound travels through the air at about 340 meters per second, which is much slower than the speed of light. This is why during a thunderstorm in Sri Lanka, you will see the flash of lightning almost instantly, but the thunder follows a few seconds later. You can calculate how far away a storm is by counting the seconds between the flash and the sound; every three seconds roughly equals one kilometer of distance.
