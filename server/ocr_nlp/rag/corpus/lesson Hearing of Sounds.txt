SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: Sound Perception Through Hearing
DEFINITION:
Sound is perceived when the ear detects vibrations traveling through a medium such as air and the brain interprets them as meaningful signals.

KEY_POINTS:

Many objects produce vibrations, but hearing is needed to perceive them as sound.
The ear acts as a receiver that captures vibrations.
The brain interprets incoming signals as speech, music, or warning sounds.

EXAMPLES:
A ringing school bell creates vibrations in air that students hear as a signal to change periods.

KEYWORDS:
sound perception, vibration, ear, hearing, brain interpretation

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: Ear Structure and Hearing Pathway
DEFINITION:
Hearing occurs through a pathway in which the pinna collects sound waves, the eardrum vibrates, middle-ear bones amplify motion, the cochlea converts it to electrical signals, and the auditory nerve carries signals to the brain.

KEY_POINTS:

The pinna collects sound and directs it into the ear canal.
The eardrum and three tiny middle-ear bones transmit and amplify vibrations.
The cochlea converts mechanical vibrations into electrical nerve signals.

EXAMPLES:
When someone speaks, their voice waves enter the ear, vibrate the eardrum, and are processed by the cochlea and auditory nerve.

KEYWORDS:
pinna, ear canal, eardrum, middle ear bones, cochlea, auditory nerve

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Human Auditory Frequency Range
DEFINITION:
The human ear hears only a specific frequency range, usually about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz in healthy young people.

KEY_POINTS:

Infrasound is below 20 Hz and is usually not heard by humans.
Ultrasound is above 20,000 Hz and is usually not heard by humans.
High-frequency hearing often decreases with age and can worsen with long-term loud-noise exposure.

EXAMPLES:
A very high-pitched dog whistle may not be heard by humans, even though dogs react.

KEYWORDS:
frequency, hertz, 20 Hz, 20000 Hz, infrasound, ultrasound

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C4
NAME: Hearing in Animals and Echolocation
DEFINITION:
Different animals hear different frequency ranges, and some species use specialized hearing behaviors such as echolocation for navigation and hunting.

KEY_POINTS:

Bats and dolphins use ultrasound echoes to detect objects and prey.
Elephants use low-frequency infrasound to communicate over long distances.
Dogs hear higher frequencies than humans.

EXAMPLES:
A bat emits high-pitched clicks and uses returning echoes to avoid obstacles in darkness.

KEYWORDS:
animal hearing, echolocation, bats, dolphins, elephants, dogs, ultrasound, infrasound

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C5
NAME: Sound Localization with Two Ears
DEFINITION:
Sound localization is the brain’s ability to find sound direction by comparing tiny differences in arrival time and loudness between the two ears.

KEY_POINTS:

Sound reaches the nearer ear slightly earlier than the farther ear.
Sound is often slightly louder in the nearer ear.
The brain combines these differences to identify direction.

EXAMPLES:
If a vehicle horn sounds from the right, the right ear receives it first and slightly louder, helping a person turn correctly.

KEYWORDS:
sound localization, two ears, time difference, loudness difference, direction

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C6
NAME: Hearing Protection and Ear Safety
DEFINITION:
Hearing protection involves preventing damage to delicate ear structures, especially the eardrum and cochlear hair cells, to avoid permanent hearing loss.

KEY_POINTS:

Long-term loud sound exposure can damage cochlear hair cells permanently.
Sharp objects or deep cotton buds can tear the eardrum.
In Sri Lanka, swimming in polluted water can increase ear infection risk, so ears should be dried and pain should be medically checked.

EXAMPLES:
Listening to very loud headphone music for long periods can reduce hearing sensitivity over time.

KEYWORDS:
hearing protection, cochlear hair cells, eardrum injury, ear infection, noise exposure, safety

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C7
NAME: Speed of Sound Compared with Light
DEFINITION:
Sound travels much slower than light, so light events are seen before their sounds are heard.

KEY_POINTS:

Sound in air travels at about 340 m/s.
Light reaches the eye almost instantly over common Earth distances.
Time delay between lightning and thunder can estimate storm distance.

EXAMPLES:
During a Sri Lankan thunderstorm, a 6-second delay between flash and thunder suggests the storm is about 2 kilometers away.

KEYWORDS:
speed of sound, speed of light, thunder, lightning, storm distance

SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M1
STATEMENT:
All vibrations in the environment can be heard by humans.

CORRECTION:
Humans hear only a specific frequency range, usually about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz in healthy young people.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M2
STATEMENT:
The ear alone identifies sounds without help from the brain.

CORRECTION:
The ear converts vibrations into electrical signals, but the brain interprets those signals as meaningful sounds.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M3
STATEMENT:
Cotton buds are always safe to insert deeply into ears.

CORRECTION:
Deep insertion can injure the ear canal or tear the eardrum, which can reduce hearing and cause medical problems.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M4
STATEMENT:
Hearing loss from loud sound is always temporary.

CORRECTION:
Damage to cochlear hair cells can be permanent because these cells do not regenerate effectively.

==================================================
SECTION: MISCONCEPTION
MISCONCEPTION_ID: M5
STATEMENT:
Lightning and thunder happen at different times.

CORRECTION:
They happen at the same event, but light arrives much faster than sound, so thunder is heard later.

SECTION: SUMMARY
Hearing happens when vibrations are collected by the ear and converted into electrical signals that the brain interprets as sound. The hearing pathway includes the pinna, ear canal, eardrum, middle-ear bones, cochlea, and auditory nerve. Humans hear only a limited frequency range, while many animals detect higher or lower frequencies and may use echolocation. Two ears help locate sound direction by comparing tiny differences in timing and loudness. Protecting hearing is essential because loud noise and ear injury can cause permanent damage, and understanding the speed difference between light and sound helps explain thunder delay and storm distance in Sri Lanka.
