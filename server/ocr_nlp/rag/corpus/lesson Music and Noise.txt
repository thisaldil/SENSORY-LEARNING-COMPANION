6.4 Music and Noise
Although everything we hear is produced by vibrations, our brain categorizes these sounds differently based on how they make us feel and the pattern of their vibrations.

1. Music
Music consists of sounds that are pleasant to the ear. These sounds are produced by regular vibrations. When an object vibrates in a steady, repeating pattern, it creates a musical note that makes us feel calm or happy.

Examples: The sound of a violin, a bamboo flute, a song sung beautifully, or the rhythmic beating of a Sabaragamuwa drum.

2. Noise
Noise consists of sounds that are unpleasant, harsh, or disturbing. These sounds are produced by irregular vibrations. When vibrations are disorganized and change suddenly, the sound becomes jarring and can cause discomfort.

Examples: The screeching of a bus brake, the sound of a jackhammer on a road, a loud explosion, or many people shouting at once in a market.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The Science of Waveforms
In physics, we can actually "see" the difference between music and noise using a machine called an oscilloscope. A musical sound produces a periodic wave, which looks like a beautiful, repeating pattern of smooth hills and valleys. Noise, however, produces a non-periodic wave, which looks like a mess of random, jagged lines. This irregular pattern is why our brain finds noise "tiresome"—it has to work much harder to process disorganized information compared to the predictable pattern of a musical rhythm.

Musical Instruments: Shaping the Vibrations
Musical instruments are carefully crafted "vibration machines." In Sri Lanka, traditional instruments are categorized by how they make music:

Percussion (Awanaddha): Like the Geta Beraya. The skin is tightened precisely so that when hit, it vibrates at a specific frequency to produce a musical tone rather than a dull thud.

Wind (Sushira): Like the Horanewa or flute. These use a column of air. By covering different holes, the player changes the length of the vibrating air, changing the note.

String (Taththa): Like a violin or guitar. The thickness and tension of the strings are set to ensure the vibrations remain regular and pleasant.

The Subjectivity of Sound
Is "Music" always music? Sometimes, what one person considers music might be noise to another. For example, very loud heavy metal music might be pleasant to a fan but "noise" to someone trying to sleep. However, in Science, we generally define noise by its intensity (loudness) and its disorganized nature. Any sound that is loud enough to cause physical pain or stress is classified as noise, regardless of whether it has a rhythm.

Noise Pollution and Health
When our environment is filled with constant, loud, unpleasant sounds, it is called Noise Pollution. This is a major problem in busy Sri Lankan cities like Colombo and Kandy.

Physical Effects: Constant noise can lead to permanent hearing loss by damaging the tiny hair cells in the cochlea of the ear.

Psychological Effects: It can cause high blood pressure, headaches, loss of sleep (insomnia), and increased stress levels.
For a student, studying in a noisy environment is difficult because the brain is constantly trying to "filter out" the irregular vibrations of the noise, leaving less energy for the brain to focus on learning.

Controlling Noise
Scientists and engineers work to turn "noise" into "quiet" through several methods:

Absorption: Using soft materials like curtains, carpets, or special foam (acoustic panels) that "soak up" vibrations so they don't bounce back.

Barriers: Building thick walls or planting rows of trees along highways. Trees are excellent "noise buffers" in Sri Lanka; their leaves vibrate and absorb the energy of traffic sounds.

Source Control: Using silencers on vehicle exhausts and ensuring machines are well-oiled so they don't create "friction noise."

The Concept of "White Noise"
An interesting fact for a RAG system to include is White Noise. This is a special kind of noise that contains all audible frequencies at once (like the sound of a fan or a radio between stations). Believe it or not, some people use this "organized noise" to help them sleep because it drowns out sudden, irregular noises like a dog barking or a door slamming!
