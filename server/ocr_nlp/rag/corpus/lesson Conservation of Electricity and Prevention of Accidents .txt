Conservation of Electricity and Prevention of Accidents
Electricity is a valuable resource that is expensive to produce. Using it carelessly not only wastes money but also harms the environment. Furthermore, because electricity is powerful energy, we must follow strict safety rules to prevent accidents like fires or electric shocks.

1. Conservation of Electricity
Conservation means using electricity only when necessary and reducing waste.

Switch off when not in use: Turn off lights, fans, and televisions when you leave a room.

Use Energy-Efficient Bulbs: Replace old filament bulbs with LED bulbs, which provide the same brightness using much less power.

Utilize Sunlight: During the day, open curtains and windows to use natural light instead of switching on electric lamps.

Iron Clothes in Batches: An electric iron uses a lot of energy to heat up; ironing many clothes at once is more efficient than doing one piece every day.

2. Prevention of Accidents
Electricity can be dangerous if it passes through the human body (Electric Shock) or if it creates enough heat to start a fire.

Never touch switches with wet hands: Water conducts electricity and can lead the current into your body.

Avoid overloading sockets: Plugging too many appliances into one socket can cause wires to overheat and start a fire.

Do not insert objects into sockets: Never put wires, hairpins, or any metal objects into the holes of a wall plug.

Stay away from fallen power lines: If you see a wire fallen on the road after a storm, do not go near it; inform an adult or the Ceylon Electricity Board (CEB) immediately.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The Hidden Cost: Why We Conserve
In Sri Lanka, a large portion of our electricity is generated by burning coal and oil. These are non-renewable resources, meaning they will eventually run out. When we burn these fuels, they release carbon dioxide, which contributes to global warming and changes our weather patterns. By conserving electricity, you are directly helping to reduce the amount of pollution released into the atmosphere. Even "Clean Energy" like hydropower needs conservation; during the dry season, our reservoirs run low, and we must save electricity to ensure there is enough for everyone until the rains return.

The Science of the Electric Shock
To understand why we need to prevent accidents, we must understand how electricity affects the body. Our nerves and muscles operate using tiny internal electrical signals. If a strong external current from a wall socket (230V in Sri Lanka) passes through you, it interferes with these signals. This can cause muscles to "lock up," making it impossible to let go of the wire. More dangerously, it can interfere with the electrical rhythm of the heart, causing it to stop. This is why "Grounding" or "Earthing" is used in homes—a third wire (the green/yellow one) provides a safe path for "stray" electricity to go into the earth rather than through a person.

Safety Devices: Fuses and Circuit Breakers
Modern homes are equipped with "invisible bodyguards" to prevent accidents.

The Fuse: A fuse contains a thin wire that melts if too much electricity flows through it. If there is a "short circuit," the fuse melts and breaks the circuit, stopping a fire before it starts.

The Trip Switch (RCCB): This is a very sensitive device that monitors the flow of electricity. If it "senses" that even a tiny bit of electricity is leaking (perhaps through a person), it "trips" and cuts off the power in a fraction of a second. This device has saved countless lives in Sri Lanka.

First Aid for Electrical Accidents
If you see someone who has been "caught" by electricity, you must act quickly but safely:

Do Not Touch Them: If you touch the person while they are still in contact with the wire, the electricity will flow into you too.

Switch off the Main Power: If possible, turn off the main switch of the house immediately.

Use an Insulator: If you cannot turn off the power, use a dry wooden stick or a plastic pipe to push the person away from the wire or push the wire away from them.

Call for Help: Dial 1990 (Suwa Seriya) for emergency medical assistance.

Phantom Power: The Silent Waster
Many electronic appliances, like TVs and microwave ovens, have a "Standby" mode. Even when the device is "off," a small red light might stay on. This is called Phantom Power. While it seems small, thousands of homes leaving devices on standby across Sri Lanka add up to a massive waste of energy. To truly conserve, you should turn the appliance off at the wall socket.

Sustainability: The "Energy Star" Rating
When buying new appliances, look for the "Energy Star" label or the Sri Lanka Sustainable Energy Authority rating. These labels tell you how efficient the machine is. A 5-star rated refrigerator uses significantly less electricity than a 1-star model. Choosing efficient appliances is one of the best long-term ways to conserve energy.
