Water, a Limited Resource
Even though our planet is called the "Blue Planet" because it is mostly covered in water, only a very small amount is actually available for us to use.

1. Distribution of Earth's Water
Salt Water: About 97% of the water on Earth is in the oceans and seas. This water is too salty for drinking, farming, or most industrial uses.

Fresh Water: Only about 3% of the total water on Earth is fresh water.

Available Fresh Water: Out of that 3%, a large portion is trapped as ice in glaciers and polar ice caps. Only a tiny fraction (less than 1%) is available in rivers, lakes, and underground for human use.

2. Why Water is Limited
Pollution: When we throw garbage, plastics, or chemicals into rivers, that water becomes unusable.

Wasteful Usage: Leaving taps running or using too much water for simple tasks reduces the amount available for others.

Climate Change: Changes in weather patterns can lead to long droughts, drying up our traditional water sources like tanks and wells.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The "One Percent" Reality
To help an 11-year-old visualize how limited water is, imagine if all the water on Earth could fit into a large 10-liter bucket.

The salt water in the oceans would fill 9.7 liters of that bucket.

The ice caps and glaciers would take up about 200 milliliters (the size of a small juice box).

The amount of water available for all the humans, animals, and plants on land would be just one tiny teaspoon!
This visualization helps us understand that while water seems abundant when we look at the sea, the water we actually need to survive is incredibly scarce.

Water Pollution: The Silent Threat
In Sri Lanka, one of the biggest reasons water is becoming "limited" is pollution.

Industrial Waste: Some factories release harmful chemicals directly into rivers like the Kelani River.

Agrochemicals: Overusing fertilizers and pesticides in farms can seep into the groundwater. In the North Central Province, this has been linked to health issues, making the well water unsafe to drink.

Domestic Waste: Plastic bottles and polythene bags thrown into drains eventually block waterways and pollute the lagoons.
When water is polluted, it is removed from the "available" category, making the limited supply even smaller.

The Impact of Deforestation
Forests act like giant sponges. When it rains in the central hills of Sri Lanka, the trees and soil soak up the water and release it slowly into rivers throughout the year. When forests are cut down, the rain runs off the surface too quickly, causing floods during the rainy season and leaving the rivers dry during the summer. Protecting our forests is a direct way of protecting our limited water resources.

Water Scarcity and Population Growth
As the number of people in the world (and in Sri Lanka) increases, the demand for water grows. We need more water for drinking, more for growing food, and more for manufacturing clothes and electronics. However, the total amount of fresh water on Earth does not increase. This creates "Water Stress," where there isn't enough clean water to meet everyone's needs. This is why many cities now have to practice "Water Cutting" or rationing during dry months.

Conservation: Saving Every Drop
Because water is limited, we must practice Water Conservation. This means using water wisely.

Home habits: Turning off the tap while brushing teeth, fixing leaking washers, and using a bucket instead of a hose to wash a bicycle.

Rainwater Harvesting: In the dry zones of Sri Lanka, many people collect rain from their roofs into large tanks. This "Atmospheric Water" can then be used for gardening or toilets, saving the precious "Groundwater" for drinking.

The Concept of "Virtual Water"
A more advanced concept for a RAG system to include is "Virtual Water." This is the water used to create the products we use. For example, it takes about 2,500 liters of water to produce just one cotton t-shirt (to grow the cotton and process the fabric). When we waste food or clothes, we are also wasting the limited water that was used to make them.
