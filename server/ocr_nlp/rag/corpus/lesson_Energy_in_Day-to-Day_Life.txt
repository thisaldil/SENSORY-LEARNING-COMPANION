What is Energy?
In our daily lives, we perform many activities such as pulling a rope, carrying a heavy load, or loading goods into a vehicle. To perform all these tasks, some work must be done. Energy is defined as the ability to do work.

2. Sources of Energy and Their Applications
We use different sources of energy to make our work easier. Below are the primary sources used in Sri Lanka and their common applications:
+2

The Sun (Solar Energy):


Applications: Used for drying clothes, drying grains (like chilies and paddy), making "Lunu dehi," and heating water in solar water heaters.
+1


Technology: Solar panels convert sunlight into electricity for home appliances and satellites.

Biomass:


Definition: Living plant or animal materials used as fuel (e.g., firewood, coconut shells, saw dust, rice bran).


Applications: Used for cooking meals and boiling water using hearths like the "saw dust stove" (kudu lipa).
+4

Fossil Fuels:


Definition: Fuels made from buried plants and animals subjected to high pressure over millions of years (e.g., coal, petroleum oil, L.P. gas).
+1


Applications: Powering vehicles (petrol/diesel), cooking (L.P. gas), and generating electricity in coal power plants.
+4

Wind:


Applications: Used by farmers to separate unfilled rice grains (winnowing), to pump water, and to grind grains in windmills.
+1


Electricity: Large wind vanes are used in places like Hambantota and Puttalam to rotate dynamos and generate electricity.
+2

Running Water:


Applications: Used in hydropower stations (like Laxapana and Victoria) where flowing water rotates large turbines to generate electricity.

The Ultimate Source: Solar Energy
While we use many different fuels, almost all energy on Earth originally comes from the Sun.
+1


Food Energy: Green plants use sunlight for photosynthesis to produce food. When animals or humans eat these plants, they are consuming stored solar energy.
+1


Stored Energy in Fuels: Biomass is made of plants that grew using sunlight. Fossil fuels like coal and oil are simply the remains of ancient plants and animals that lived millions of years ago, meaning they are also "stored" solar energy.
+1

Renewable vs. Non-Renewable Energy
For a modern student, it is important to distinguish between how "limited" these resources are:

Renewable Energy: These sources do not run out. Sunlight, wind, and running water are examples. In Sri Lanka, we are lucky to have plenty of sunlight and rain, which allows us to use hydropower and solar power as sustainable options.

Non-Renewable Energy: Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) are limited. Once we burn them, they are gone forever because they take millions of years to form. Since Sri Lanka imports petroleum from other countries, using it efficiently is very important for our economy.
+1

Energy Transformation: A Science Secret
Energy is never "created" from nothing; it only changes from one form to another.

In a Flashlight, the chemical energy in the dry cell (battery) turns into light energy.

In a Wind Turbine, the motion energy of the wind turns into electrical energy.

In a Solar Cooker, the light energy from the sun is concentrated to become heat energy to boil water.

The Story of Electricity in Sri Lanka
Electricity is one of the most useful forms of energy because it can be easily sent through wires to our homes. Sri Lanka has a rich history with energy; Mr. D.J. Wimalasurendra was the first to realize that our waterfalls and rivers could be used to generate hydropower. Today, we use a mix of sources (Hydro, Wind, Coal, and Fuel) to keep our lights on and our factories running.

Heat Energy and the Environment
Heat is a form of energy we feel as warmth. The Sun is our main source of heat. While we need heat to live, too much heat can be dangerous. Changes in the Earth's temperature can lead to natural disasters like floods or droughts. This is why scientists study "Weather and Climate"—to understand how energy from the sun interacts with our air and water.
+4

Energy Conservation: Why it Matters
Because some energy sources are limited and others can be expensive, we must use energy productively. Simple habits like switching off unnecessary lights, using "saw dust stoves" instead of expensive gas when possible, and maintaining vehicles to save fuel can make a big difference. For an 11-year-old, "saving energy" means there will be more resources available for the future and less pollution for our environment.
