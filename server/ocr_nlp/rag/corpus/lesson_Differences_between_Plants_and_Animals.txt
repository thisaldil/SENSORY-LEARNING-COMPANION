While both plants and animals are living organisms that grow, breathe, and reproduce, they have very distinct ways of surviving in their environment. 

1. Movement
Animals: Most animals can move their entire body from one place to another. This is called locomotion. They move to find food, escape danger, or find shelter.

Plants: Plants are generally fixed to one spot by their roots. They do not show locomotion. However, they show movements of parts, such as flowers opening or leaves turning toward the sun.

2. Nutrition (How they get food)
Animals: Animals cannot make their own food. They must eat plants or other animals to get energy. 
+1


Plants: Green plants make their own food using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide through a process called photosynthesis.

3. Sensory Organs

Animals: Animals have well-developed sensory organs like eyes, ears, and a nose to respond quickly to their surroundings. 

Plants: Plants do not have nervous systems or sense organs like animals, though they still respond slowly to light and gravity.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The Biological "Engines": Comparing Energy Use
The fundamental difference between plants and animals lies in their energy strategies. Animals are known as consumers (or heterotrophs). Because they move actively, they require a high amount of energy quickly. This is why animals have complex systems like a heart to pump blood and lungs to take in large amounts of oxygen.

Plants, on the other hand, are producers (or autotrophs). Since they do not move from place to place, their energy needs are lower and more consistent. Instead of a heart, they use a system of tiny tubes called Xylem and Phloem to transport water and food slowly throughout their bodies. In the Sri Lankan context, think of a massive Jak tree; it stays in one place for decades, slowly building its trunk, whereas a Squirrel living in that same tree is constantly darting around, requiring immediate "fuel" from the Jak fruit. 

Growth Patterns: Indeterminate vs. Determinate
A very interesting difference for a Grade 6 student is how they grow. Animals usually have determinate growth. This means they grow until they reach a certain size and age, then they stop. A human child will eventually stop getting taller.

Plants often have indeterminate growth. As long as they have the right conditions (water, sunlight, and space), many plants can keep growing new branches and leaves throughout their entire lives. This is why you can prune a hibiscus hedge in your garden, and it will simply grow new shoots to replace the old ones. Most animals cannot regrow a limb if it is lost.

Cellular Structure: The Invisible Wall
If we looked through a compound microscope, like the one used to see micro-organisms, we would see a massive difference at the cellular level.  Animal cells are flexible and soft, which allows for movement and different body shapes. Plant cells, however, are encased in a "Cell Wall" made of a tough material called cellulose. This cell wall acts like a skeleton for the plant, giving it the strength to stand tall without having bones. This is why wood is hard and a dog's fur is soft.

Respiration and Gas Exchange
While both plants and animals breathe (respire) to produce energy, they interact with the air differently. Animals breathe in oxygen and breathe out carbon dioxide constantly. Plants do the same during respiration; however, during the day, they also perform photosynthesis. During photosynthesis, they take in the carbon dioxide that animals breathe out and release the oxygen that animals need. This beautiful "give and take" relationship is what keeps the atmosphere of the Earth balanced.

Defense Mechanisms
Because animals can move, their primary defense is often "fight or flight"—they either run away or fight back. Since plants cannot run, they have developed amazing physical and chemical defenses. Some plants have thorns (like a rose or a bougainvillea), while others produce chemicals that taste bitter or are poisonous to keep animals from eating them.
