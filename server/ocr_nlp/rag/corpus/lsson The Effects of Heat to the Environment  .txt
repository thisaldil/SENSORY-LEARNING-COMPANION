Effects of Heat to the Environment
Heat is the "engine" of the environment. While we need heat for life, too much or too little of it can cause drastic changes to the world around us.

1. Warming of the Environment
During the day, the Sun heats the Earth's surface. At night, the Earth releases some of this heat back into space. This cycle keeps the temperature within a range where plants and animals can survive. However, if the environment traps too much heat, it leads to Global Warming, which is the gradual increase in the Earth's overall temperature.

2. Impact on Living Beings
Animals react differently to environmental heat based on their biology:

Homeothermic (Warm-blooded): Animals like humans, birds, and dogs can maintain a steady body temperature even if the environment changes.

Poikilothermic (Cold-blooded): Animals like frogs, snakes, and lizards cannot regulate their own heat. Their body temperature changes with the environment. If it gets too hot, they must hide in the shade or underground to survive.

3. Influence on Weather and Water
Heat drives the Water Cycle. It causes water from our rivers and the Indian Ocean to evaporate and turn into clouds.

Droughts: Excessive heat for a long time causes water sources to dry up, killing crops and making it difficult for people and animals in dry zones like Hambantota or Polonnaruwa to find drinking water.

Extreme Storms: When the ocean gets too warm, it provides extra energy for powerful storms and cyclones, which can damage homes and trees.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The "Greenhouse Effect" and Our Atmosphere
To understand environmental heat, imagine the Earth is inside a giant glass greenhouse. The atmosphere contains certain gases like Carbon Dioxide and Methane that act like the glass. They let sunlight in but stop all the heat from escaping. This is the Greenhouse Effect. Without it, the Earth would be a frozen ball of ice!
+1

The problem today is that humans are burning too much coal, oil, and forest wood, which adds extra greenhouse gases to the "glass roof." This traps too much heat, making the planet warmer than it should be. This is why we see glaciers in the North and South Poles melting, which causes the sea level to rise. For an island like Sri Lanka, rising sea levels are a serious concern because they can lead to coastal flooding.
+2

Thermal Expansion of the Oceans
As we learned in section 9.2, heat causes substances to expand. This applies to the ocean too! When the atmosphere warms up, the top layers of the ocean soak up that heat and physically expand. Scientists estimate that about half of the sea-level rise we see today is not just from melting ice, but from the water itself getting warmer and taking up more space.

Heat and the Migration of Species
When the environment gets too hot, animals and plants "move." In Sri Lanka, you might find certain birds or insects moving from the hot dry lowlands up into the cooler hills of Nuwara Eliya to find a comfortable temperature. However, some species cannot move fast enough. If a mountain becomes too warm, the plants at the very top have nowhere else to go and may become extinct. This is why protecting the "Cloud Forests" of Sri Lanka is so important for preserving our unique wildlife.

The "Urban Heat Island" Effect
Have you noticed that it feels much hotter in the middle of a busy city like Colombo than it does in a rural village with many trees? This is called the Urban Heat Island effect. Concrete buildings and asphalt roads soak up the Sun's heat during the day and release it slowly at night. Trees, on the other hand, provide shade and release water vapor through their leaves (transpiration), which cools the air down. This is a great reason to plant more trees in our schools and cities!
+1

Heat and the Timing of Nature
Nature follows a "clock" based on temperature. Many trees in Sri Lanka, like the beautiful Tabebuia (Pink Trumpet) or the Flame Tree, bloom when it gets hot. Some birds begin to nest when they feel the temperature rising. If the environment heats up too early in the year, it can confuse these animals. A bird might hatch its chicks before the insects they eat are ready, leading to a shortage of food for the babies.

What Can We Do?
Even as Grade 6 students, you can help manage environmental heat:

Plant Trees: Trees are "natural air conditioners" that cool the environment and soak up greenhouse gases.

Save Energy: As we learned in Unit 8, generating electricity often involves burning fuels that heat the Earth. Switching off a bulb helps cool the planet!

Reduce Waste: Manufacturing new things uses heat energy. By recycling, we save that energy.
