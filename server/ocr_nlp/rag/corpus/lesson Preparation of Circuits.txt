SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C1
NAME: Electric Circuit as a Complete Loop
DEFINITION:
An electric circuit is a closed loop path through which electric energy flows from a source, through components, and back to the source.

KEY_POINTS:

A circuit works only when the loop is complete.
Electricity moves from one terminal of the cell through the load and returns to the other terminal.
If the loop is broken anywhere, energy flow stops.

EXAMPLES:
A bulb in a classroom setup lights only when wire connections form a complete path between both cell terminals.

KEYWORDS:
electric circuit, closed loop, current path, energy flow, complete connection

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C2
NAME: Components of a Simple Circuit
DEFINITION:
A simple circuit needs a power source, conductors, a load, and often a switch to control operation.

KEY_POINTS:

The cell or battery supplies electrical energy.
Copper wires conduct electricity between components.
The load (bulb or buzzer) uses electrical energy to produce light or sound.
The switch opens or closes the path to control the load.

EXAMPLES:
A school experiment uses one dry cell, two wires, a small bulb, and a switch mounted on a board.

KEYWORDS:
power source, cell, conductor, copper wire, load, bulb, buzzer, switch

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C3
NAME: Open Circuit and Closed Circuit
DEFINITION:
A closed circuit allows electricity to flow, while an open circuit has a break that stops flow.

KEY_POINTS:

Switch ON usually means closed circuit and working load.
Switch OFF usually means open circuit and non-working load.
Disconnected wires or poor contacts create open circuits even if the switch is ON.

EXAMPLES:
When one wire is removed from a bulb holder, the bulb goes off because the circuit is open.

KEYWORDS:
open circuit, closed circuit, switch ON, switch OFF, continuity

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C4
NAME: Switch Operation and Insulating Air Gap
DEFINITION:
A switch controls current by bringing metal contacts together or separating them with an insulating air gap.

KEY_POINTS:

In ON position, metal contacts touch and allow electron flow.
In OFF position, contacts separate and air blocks easy current flow.
Switches improve safety and reduce unnecessary energy use.

EXAMPLES:
Pressing a switch closes metal contacts so a bulb lights instantly; releasing it opens the path and the bulb turns off.

KEYWORDS:
switch contacts, metal connection, air gap, insulator, safety control

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C5
NAME: Series and Parallel Circuit Connections
DEFINITION:
Multiple loads can be connected in series (single path) or parallel (multiple branches), giving different behavior when one load fails.

KEY_POINTS:

Series circuits have one path; one broken component stops all loads.
Parallel circuits have separate branches; other branches continue if one fails.
Homes in Sri Lanka use parallel wiring so rooms work independently.

EXAMPLES:
If the kitchen bulb fails in a house, bedroom lights still work because house circuits are parallel.

KEYWORDS:
series circuit, parallel circuit, branch, household wiring, independent load

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C6
NAME: Voltage, Resistance, and Bulb Brightness
DEFINITION:
Bulb brightness depends on voltage pushing current and resistance opposing current inside circuit components.

KEY_POINTS:

Higher voltage provides greater electrical push.
Resistance limits current and can convert electrical energy into heat.
Bulb filaments glow because electrical energy becomes heat and then light in high-resistance material.

EXAMPLES:
A 9V battery can make a suitable bulb brighter than a 1.5V cell, while filament resistance controls heating and glow.

KEYWORDS:
voltage, resistance, filament, brightness, electrical pressure, energy conversion

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C7
NAME: Short Circuit and Protective Devices
DEFINITION:
A short circuit happens when current takes a very low-resistance path directly between battery terminals, causing dangerous overheating.

KEY_POINTS:

Bypassing the load removes normal resistance in the path.
Excess current can overheat wires, melt insulation, and start fires.
Fuses and circuit breakers cut power automatically during dangerous current conditions.

EXAMPLES:
Directly joining positive and negative battery terminals with wire causes rapid heating; a circuit breaker in home wiring interrupts such faults.

KEYWORDS:
short circuit, overheating, fuse, circuit breaker, electrical safety, fault current

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C8
NAME: Circuit Symbols as Scientific Language
DEFINITION:
Circuit symbols are standardized drawings used worldwide to represent electrical components and connections.

KEY_POINTS:

A cell is shown by long and short parallel lines.
A bulb is shown as a circle with a cross.
A switch is shown as a breakable line contact.
Standard symbols allow people in different countries to read the same diagram.

EXAMPLES:
A student in Sri Lanka can interpret a circuit diagram drawn by an engineer in Japan using common symbols.

KEYWORDS:
circuit symbols, diagram, standard notation, cell symbol, bulb symbol, switch symbol

==================================================
SECTION: CORE_CONCEPT
CONCEPT_ID: C9
NAME: Conductors, Insulators, and Wire Stripping
DEFINITION:
Conductors allow current flow while insulators resist it, so exposed metal contact is needed at connection points.

KEY_POINTS:

Copper is a conductor used inside wires.
Plastic and rubber are insulators used for protection.
Wire ends must be stripped so metal touches metal at terminals.
Insulating gloves and boots protect electricians from electric shock.

EXAMPLES:
If plastic insulation covers wire tips at a battery terminal, the circuit stays open and the bulb does not light.

KEYWORDS:
conductor, insulator, copper, plastic insulation, wire stripping, rubber safety gear

SECTION: SUMMARY
A simple electric circuit works only when all required components—power source, conductor, load, and usually a switch—are connected as a complete loop. In a closed circuit, current flows and devices work, while in an open circuit, any break stops flow. Switches control current by joining or separating metal contacts, and air gaps act as insulation in the OFF state. Multiple loads can be connected in series or parallel; series has one path so one failure stops all loads, while parallel has branches so other loads continue, as in Sri Lankan household wiring. Bulb brightness depends on voltage and resistance, and filament resistance converts electrical energy to heat and light. Short circuits are dangerous low-resistance faults that can overheat wires, so fuses and circuit breakers are essential protection. Standard circuit symbols form a universal scientific language, and understanding conductors and insulators—including proper wire stripping and protective rubber gear—supports both correct circuit construction and safety.
