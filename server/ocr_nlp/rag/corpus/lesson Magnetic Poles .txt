Magnetic Poles
Every magnet, regardless of its shape or size, has two specific regions where the magnetic force is the strongest. These regions are called Poles.

1. Identifying the Poles
A magnet has two poles:

North Pole (N): Usually colored red or marked with an "N."

South Pole (S): Usually colored blue/black or marked with an "S."

If you dip a bar magnet into a pile of iron filings, you will notice that most of the filings stick to the two ends. Very few filings stick to the middle. This proves that the magnetic strength is concentrated at the poles.

2. The First Law of Magnetism
The way poles interact with each other follows a very strict rule:

Like poles repel: If you bring a North pole near another North pole, they will push each other away. The same happens with two South poles.

Unlike poles attract: If you bring a North pole near a South pole, they will pull toward each other and stick together.

Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)
The Concept of the "Freely Suspended" Magnet
One of the most important characteristics of magnetic poles is how they react to the Earth. If you hang a bar magnet by a thin thread so it can swing freely, it will always come to rest pointing in a North-South direction.

The end that points toward the Earth's geographic North is called the North-seeking pole (or simply the North Pole).

The end that points toward the Earth's geographic South is called the South-seeking pole (or simply the South Pole).
This is the scientific principle behind the magnetic compass, which has been used by Sri Lankan sailors for centuries to find their way across the Indian Ocean.

Magnetic Poles Always Come in Pairs
A very interesting fact about magnets is that you can never have a "monopole" (a single pole). If you take a bar magnet and break it exactly in half, you might think you would have one North piece and one South piece. However, science shows that you will actually end up with two smaller magnets, each having its own North and South pole. Even if you keep breaking it into tiny pieces, every single piece will still have two poles.

The Earth’s Magnetic Poles vs. Geographic Poles
It is important to understand that the Earth acts like a giant bar magnet buried inside. However, there is a "science secret" here: the Earth's Magnetic North Pole is actually near the geographic South Pole, and the Earth's Magnetic South Pole is near the geographic North Pole. Because "unlike poles attract," the North pole of your compass needle is pulled toward the Earth's Magnetic South Pole (which is located at the geographic North). This is why your compass "points North"!

Magnetic Induction: Creating Temporary Poles
When you bring a strong magnet close to a magnetic material like an iron nail, the nail becomes a magnet itself for a short time. This is called Magnetic Induction. The end of the nail closest to the magnet's North pole will become a South pole, and the far end will become a North pole. This explains why a magnet can pick up a chain of paperclips; each paperclip becomes a temporary magnet with its own poles, passing the force down the line.

The Strength of Poles in Different Shapes
The position of poles changes based on the shape of the magnet:

Bar Magnet: Poles are at the two opposite ends.

Horseshoe Magnet: The "U" shape brings both the North and South poles to the same side. This makes the magnet very useful for lifting heavy objects because both poles can pull on the object at the same time.

Ring Magnet: Usually, one flat face is the North pole and the other flat face is the South pole.

Cylindrical Magnet: The poles are usually at the flat circular ends.

Demagnetization: When Poles Disappear
The "poles" are created because the tiny atoms inside the magnet are all lined up facing the same way. If this alignment is messed up, the poles disappear, and the object is no longer a magnet. This can happen if you:

Heat the magnet: High heat makes atoms vibrate wildly and lose their order.

Hammer the magnet: Strong physical shocks can jumble the atoms.

Store them incorrectly: If you leave two North poles touching for a long time, they can weaken each other.
