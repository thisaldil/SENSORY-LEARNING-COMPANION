 Food ChainsA food chain always starts with a producer and follows a single path as animals eat plants or other animals.1. The Components of a Food ChainThe Sun: The ultimate source of energy for all food chains.Producers: Green plants that make their own food using sunlight.Consumers: Animals that get their energy by eating plants or other animals.Primary Consumer: An animal that eats plants (Herbivore).Secondary Consumer: An animal that eats the primary consumer (Carnivore).Tertiary Consumer: A top predator that eats the secondary consumer.2. The Direction of the ArrowIn a food chain, we use arrows $(\rightarrow)$ to connect the organisms.Important: The arrow does not mean "eats." The arrow means "is eaten by" or "energy flows to." For example: Grass $\rightarrow$ Deer means the energy from the grass flows into the deer.Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)The Flow of EnergyEnergy enters the food chain through Photosynthesis. Green plants capture sunlight and convert it into chemical energy stored in their leaves, stems, and fruits. When a herbivore, like a cow or a grasshopper, eats the plant, it takes in that stored energy. However, the animal uses up most of that energy to breathe, move, and keep its body warm. Only a small amount is stored in its body to be passed on to the next animal. This is why food chains are usually short (rarely more than 5 links)—eventually, there isn't enough energy left to support another level of consumer.Trophic LevelsEach step in a food chain is called a Trophic Level.First Trophic Level: Producers (Plants).Second Trophic Level: Primary Consumers (Herbivores).Third Trophic Level: Secondary Consumers (Small Carnivores).Fourth Trophic Level: Tertiary Consumers (Top Predators).In Sri Lanka, a paddy field food chain might look like this:Paddy Plant (Producer) $\rightarrow$ Rat (Primary Consumer) $\rightarrow$ Cobra (Secondary Consumer) $\rightarrow$ Peacock (Tertiary Consumer).The Role of DecomposersWhile standard food chains end with a top predator, there is a "hidden" final step. When the peacock or the plant dies, Decomposers (like fungi and bacteria) break down their bodies. They turn the organic matter back into minerals and nutrients in the soil. These nutrients help the plants grow, starting the chain all over again. Without decomposers, the Earth would be covered in dead matter, and plants would run out of nutrients.Aquatic Food ChainsFood chains don't just exist on land; they are very important in the oceans surrounding Sri Lanka.Phytoplankton: Microscopic plants that float in the water (Producers).Zooplankton: Tiny animals that eat the phytoplankton (Primary Consumers).Small Fish: Like Salaya or Hurulla (Secondary Consumers).Large Fish/Mammals: Like Tuna or Blue Whales (Tertiary Consumers).Balance in the Food ChainIf one link in the chain is removed, the entire system struggles. For example, if farmers kill all the snakes in a village, the number of rats will increase because nothing is eating them. These rats will then eat all the paddy crops, leaving no food for humans. This is called an Ecological Imbalance. Understanding food chains helps us realize that every animal, even a small insect or a snake, has an important job to do in nature.Bio-magnification (Advanced Concept)Food chains also show us how pollution moves. If a lake is polluted with a chemical like mercury, the small plants soak it up. Small fish eat many plants, and big fish eat many small fish. The poison "piles up" or concentrates as it moves up the chain. This is why top predators (and humans who eat them) are often at the highest risk from environmental pollution.
