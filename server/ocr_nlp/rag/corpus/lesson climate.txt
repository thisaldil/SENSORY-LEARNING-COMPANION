ClimateClimate is the average weather condition of a specific area over a very long period, usually 30 years or more. While weather changes from hour to hour, climate is stable and predictable.1. Factors Defining ClimateThe climate of a region is determined by several factors:Distance from the Equator: Places near the equator are generally hotter.Height above Sea Level (Altitude): Higher places are cooler.Proximity to the Sea: The ocean helps keep temperatures from becoming too extreme.Rainfall Patterns: How much rain a place gets and during which months.2. Climatic Zones of Sri LankaEven though Sri Lanka is a small island, we have different climatic zones:The Wet Zone: Receives high rainfall from both monsoons (e.g., Colombo, Kandy).The Dry Zone: Receives rain mainly during the North-East monsoon and has long dry periods (e.g., Anuradhapura, Jaffna).The Intermediate Zone: Fits between the wet and dry zones (e.g., Kurunegala).The Hill Country: Has a much cooler climate due to its high altitude (e.g., Nuwara Eliya).Additional Knowledge for Enhanced Learning (500+ Words)The Altitude Effect: Why is Nuwara Eliya Cold?You might wonder why Nuwara Eliya is so chilly while Colombo is hot, even though they are both in the same country. This is because of Altitude. As you go higher up into the atmosphere, the air pressure drops and the air molecules are more spread out. This causes the temperature to decrease by about $6.5^{\circ}\text{C}$ for every 1,000 meters you climb. This is why the "Hill Country" climate is perfect for growing tea, while the "Low Country" climate is better for rubber and coconut.The Role of the OceanSri Lanka’s climate is described as Tropical Maritime. Because we are an island surrounded by the Indian Ocean, the water acts like a giant temperature regulator. Water takes a long time to heat up and a long time to cool down. During the day, the ocean stays cooler than the land, and the "Sea Breeze" blows in to cool us down. This prevents Sri Lanka from becoming as hot as a desert in the middle of a continent.Monsoons: The Heartbeat of our ClimateOur climate is dominated by two major wind patterns called Monsoons:South-West Monsoon (Yala Season): From May to September, moisture-heavy winds blow from the Indian Ocean. They hit the central mountains and pour rain on the South and West of the island.North-East Monsoon (Maha Season): From December to February, winds blow from the Bay of Bengal, bringing rain to the entire island, especially the North and East.Our farmers depend on these predictable climate cycles to know when to start the Yala and Maha cultivation seasons.The Impact of Climate on LifeClimate dictates almost everything about how we live:Architecture: Houses in the dry zone often have thick walls and small windows to keep out the heat, while houses in the wet zone have sloped roofs to let rainwater run off quickly.Agriculture: You won't find many paddy fields in the highest parts of the hills because the climate is too cold for rice; instead, you see carrots, leeks, and tea.Wildlife: The animals in the dry zone, like the Sri Lankan Elephant, are adapted to finding water in small tanks (Wewas) during the dry season.Climate Change: A Global ChallengeToday, the Earth's climate is changing faster than ever before. This is called Global Climate Change. Because humans are burning fuels and cutting down forests, the Earth is trapping more heat. For Sri Lanka, this means our predictable monsoons are becoming unpredictable. We might have floods when we expect sun, or droughts when we expect rain. This makes it harder for our farmers and affects our food supply.Measuring the History of ClimateHow do we know what the climate was like 1,000 years ago? Scientists look at "Climate Proxies." These include:Tree Rings: Wide rings mean a warm, rainy year; narrow rings mean a dry, cold year.Ice Cores: Air bubbles trapped deep in the ice of the North Pole tell us what the atmosphere was like thousands of years ago.Corals: The skeletons of coral reefs near Hikkaduwa or Trincomalee store information about the ocean temperature from the past.
